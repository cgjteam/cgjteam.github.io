# Log-016
# Reconstructing Suppes' Midsegment Theorem

**Date:** 2026-07-22
**Project:** CGJteam Lab
**Status:** Constructive Affine Geometry

---

## 1. Overview

The current stage of the project focuses on reconstructing Patrick Suppes'
proof of the classical Midsegment Theorem from his paper

> *Quantifier-Free Axioms for Constructive Affine Plane Geometry*
> (Synthese, 2000).

Unlike the synthetic development used elsewhere in our geometry library,
Suppes does not begin with parallel lines or congruence.

Instead, his geometry is built from only three primitive notions:

- the midpoint operation `⊕`,
- the doubling operation `*`,
- the ternary collinearity relation `L`.

All remaining geometric concepts are constructed from these primitives. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

---

## 2. Midpoint Geometry

The central object of Suppes' affine geometry is the midpoint operation

```
a ⊕ b
```

together with the doubling operation

```
a * b.
```

These two operations satisfy a collection of quantifier-free axioms,
including idempotency, commutativity, bicommutativity, cancellation,
reduction and the linearity properties of midpoint constructions.

Rather than introducing existential axioms, Suppes replaces them by
explicit constructive operations.

This makes every geometric construction algorithmic.

---

## 3. Primitive Parallelograms

Parallelism is not introduced as a primitive relation.

Instead, Suppes defines a primitive parallelogram by combining

- non-collinearity of three vertices, and
- coincidence of the midpoints of the two diagonals.

Formally,

```
P(abcd)
    iff
T(abc)  and  a ⊕ c = b ⊕ d.
```

This definition replaces the classical affine notion of a parallelogram
by a purely midpoint-based characterization. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

---

## 4. The Midsegment Theorem

The classical Midsegment Theorem appears in the paper as Theorem 11.

Its statement is stronger than the usual formulation.

Rather than proving only that the segment joining two midpoints is
parallel to the third side, Suppes proves that the four constructed
points form a primitive parallelogram,

```
P((a ⊕ b)(b ⊕ c)(a ⊕ c)a).
```

The familiar parallelism is therefore obtained through the geometry of
primitive parallelograms rather than introduced directly. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

---

## 5. Lean Reconstruction

Our Lean formalization follows the structure of Suppes' proof as closely
as possible.

The proof is expressed entirely in terms of

- the midpoint operation,
- the doubling operation,
- collinearity,
- primitive parallelograms.

No additional geometric primitives are introduced.

The resulting formalization therefore mirrors the constructive philosophy
of the original paper.

---

## 6. Current Status

At the present stage all midpoint identities required by Suppes'
Theorem 11 have been formalized.

The remaining dependency is the cyclic permutation theorem for primitive
parallelograms (Theorem 9), which is used by Suppes in the final step of
the proof.

After this theorem is formalized, the Lean proof of the Midsegment
Theorem will coincide completely with the proof presented in the paper. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}
