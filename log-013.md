# Log 013 — From Proof to Formal Proof

### Introduction
A mathematical proof and its formal verification are not the same object. A classical proof is written for a human reader; it omits routine arguments, leaves some constructions implicit, and assumes familiarity with standard geometric reasoning. A formal proof assistant accepts none of these conventions. Every object, every construction, and every inference must be stated explicitly. For this reason, formalization is not a transcription of a proof—it is a reconstruction of its logical structure.

### The Classical vs. Formal View
In a **synthetic proof**, arguments are presented as continuous narratives. Intermediate observations are often omitted, and phrases like "Clearly..." or "It follows immediately..." are perfectly acceptable. The human reader supplies the missing details via intuition.

In a **formal proof**, every inference must be justified. Each theorem depends on previously established results, and every construction must satisfy its defining properties. Nothing is inferred from intuition; the proof becomes an explicit dependency graph rather than a narrative.

### Finlay's Construction
Ian Finlay's proof illustrates this distinction particularly well. The published argument occupies only a few paragraphs, yet its formal counterpart will necessarily contain many more intermediate statements. These additional steps do not strengthen the underlying mathematics—they simply make explicit what is already implicit in the classical proof.

### The Role of CGJteam Lab
The purpose of this project is not merely to formalize existing proofs, but to understand what must be made explicit *before* formalization becomes possible. The logical analysis performed in the previous logs provides exactly this intermediate layer, connecting the language of classical geometry with the rigorous requirements of formal verification.
