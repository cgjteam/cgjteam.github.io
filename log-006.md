# Log 006 - Reverse Engineering the Geometry Core

## Understanding the Logical Structure Behind a Formal Geometry Library

### Introduction

The completion of Finlay's synthetic proof marked the first milestone of the CGJteam Lab project.

The formal proof was correct.

The Lean code compiled.

The main mathematical argument had been successfully transferred into a proof assistant.

However, the completion of the proof revealed a new problem.

A formal library is not understood simply by reading the final theorem.

The real structure of the development is hidden inside definitions, primitive relations, auxiliary lemmas and the dependencies between them.

The next stage of the project therefore changed direction.

Instead of immediately adding new geometric results, the focus moved toward understanding the existing geometry core.

---

## From Building Proofs to Reading Libraries

A classical mathematical proof is usually presented as a sequence of ideas.

A formal proof contains additional information:

- the exact logical dependencies,
- the chosen definitions,
- the required assumptions,
- the interfaces between different parts of the theory.

This information is often invisible in ordinary mathematical writing.

A theorem such as

```lean
Finlay
```

appears mathematically simple:

the three medians of a triangle are concurrent.

However, its formal realization depends on a chain of intermediate concepts:

- midpoint,
- collinearity,
- parallelism,
- intersection,
- parallelogram structure,
- diagonal bisection,
- median construction.

Understanding this dependency graph became an independent research task.

---

## The Geometry Structure

The central object of the current development is the structure:

```lean
Geo
```

It contains the primitive geometric relations used by the library.

Among them:

```lean
Point
Collinear
Parallel
Between
Congruent
IsMidpoint
IsMedian
IsParallelogram
```

At first sight this structure may appear incomplete compared with classical synthetic geometry.

There are no coordinates.

There are no angles.

There are no distances.

There is no explicit line object.

This is not a deficiency.

It reflects an important design decision:

the library works directly with geometric relations rather than introducing unnecessary representations.

The formalization process therefore asks a fundamental question:

Which notions are actually required by a given proof?

---

## The Midpoint Investigation

One of the first observations concerned the notion of midpoint.

In elementary geometry, a midpoint is often understood through several simultaneous ideas:

- belonging to a segment,
- dividing it into two equal parts,
- lying between the endpoints.

However, the Finlay proof does not use all of these properties.

The formal development only requires the consequences:

- midpoint relations,
- collinearity,
- transfer to parallel constructions.

This revealed an important distinction:

the intuitive meaning of a geometric concept is often richer than the logical content required by a particular theorem.

Formalization forces these two levels to be separated.

---

## Collinearity as a Fundamental Relation

The role of collinearity became particularly visible during the formalization.

Many informal geometric statements use phrases such as:

"the point lies on the same line".

In the formal library this becomes:

```lean
Geo.Collinear A B C
```

A large part of the proof consists not in creating new points, but in transforming and transporting collinearity information.

For example, Finlay's construction requires changing:

```lean
Geo.Collinear C G F
```

into:

```lean
Geo.Collinear C F G
```

through the elementary transformation:

```lean
collinear_swap
```

This small example illustrates a general principle:

formal geometry is not only about geometric objects, but also about managing the logical form in which geometric information is expressed.

---

## The Role of Parallelism

Parallelism provides another example.

The informal statement:

"these two lines are parallel"

has several possible formal representations.

The current library treats parallelism as a relation between pairs of points:

```lean
Geo.Parallel A B C D
```

This choice avoids introducing separate line objects.

However, it also means that orientation changes become explicit operations.

Theorems such as:

```lean
ParallelSymm
ParallelFlipRight
```

are therefore not merely technical conveniences.

They form part of the interface through which geometric reasoning is expressed.

---

## Discovering Mathematical Interfaces

The reverse engineering process revealed that a formal library has several layers.

A simplified view of the current development is:

```
Primitive geometric relations

        |
        v

Elementary transformations

        |
        v

Reusable synthetic mechanisms

        |
        v

Complete geometric arguments
```

For the Finlay proof this becomes:

```
Collinear / Midpoint / Parallel

        |
        v

MidsegmentTheorem
ParallelCollinearLeft

        |
        v

FinlayStep1 - FinlayStep5

        |
        v

Finlay
```

The important observation is that the intermediate layer is not accidental.

It is the beginning of a reusable mathematical interface.

---

## Toward a Geometry Theory

At this stage the project does not attempt to define a complete synthetic geometry system.

The goal is different.

The aim is to understand how geometric theories emerge from formal developments.

A classical theory is usually presented from the top:

definitions first, then theorems.

A formal library often develops from the bottom:

proofs reveal missing concepts, repeated arguments reveal interfaces, and interfaces gradually become theories.

This reverse direction is one of the most interesting aspects of formal mathematics.

---

## Outlook

The next stages of CGJteam Lab will continue this investigation.

The immediate goals are:

1. Analyze the existing geometric primitives.
2. Identify stable mathematical interfaces.
3. Separate essential concepts from historical proof artifacts.
4. Develop new theories only where the formal structure justifies them.

The project therefore moves from proving individual results toward understanding the architecture of formal synthetic geometry.
