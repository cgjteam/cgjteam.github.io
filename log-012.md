# Log 012 — A Proof as a Directed Dependency Graph

### Introduction
Classical geometry presents proofs as linear sequences of statements. A reader follows one sentence after another until the theorem is established. From the perspective of formal verification, however, this representation is not fundamental. Every proof can instead be viewed as a directed graph whose vertices are mathematical statements and whose edges represent logical dependencies. This viewpoint provides a different understanding of geometric reasoning.

### From Text to Graph
A written proof suggests a chronological order, while a dependency graph represents a logical order. These two orders are generally different:
* Several independent statements may appear consecutively in a text while occupying different branches of the dependency graph.
* Conversely, statements separated by many paragraphs may be adjacent in the logical structure.

### Finlay's Construction
The proof proposed by Ian Finlay illustrates this distinction particularly well. While the written argument appears almost linear, the logical structure is not. Several branches evolve independently before merging into the final conclusion:
* The construction of the auxiliary point $P$.
* The application of the Midsegment Theorem.
* The construction of the parallelogram.

These are separate logical branches that later converge.

### Why Does This Matter?
A proof assistant does not verify a narrative; it verifies a dependency graph. Each theorem, lemma, and construction must be available before any statement depending on it can be established. The graph therefore represents the mathematical object that is actually verified, whereas the written proof is only one possible presentation of that object.

### Research Perspective
Viewing proofs as dependency graphs suggests a new way of studying classical geometry. Instead of comparing proofs by their length, one may compare them by the structure of their dependency graphs. Questions about complexity, modularity, and minimality become natural objects of investigation.
