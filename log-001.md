# Log-001 — Formalizing Finlay's Synthetic Proof

**Date:** 2026-07-11  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** First Milestone

---

## 1. Beginning

The initial objective of the project was deliberately modest.

Rather than attempting to formalize Euclidean geometry as a whole, the goal was to verify a single elegant result: Ian Finlay's synthetic proof that the three medians of a triangle are concurrent.

At first sight this appears to be a self-contained exercise in formal verification. During the development, however, it gradually became clear that the proof itself suggests the structure of the library required to support it.

The geometry library therefore was not designed first. It emerged from the proof.

---

## 2. Finlay's Argument

Let △ABC be a triangle.

Let E and F be the midpoints of AC and AB, respectively, and let

    G = BE ∩ CF.

To prove that G is the centroid of △ABC, Finlay introduces an auxiliary point P such that G is the midpoint of AP.

The argument then proceeds through a remarkably short sequence of geometric observations.

Since F and G are the midpoints of AB and AP,

    FG ∥ BP.

Because C, F and G are collinear,

    CG ∥ BP.

Similarly, since E and G are the midpoints of AC and AP,

    EG ∥ CP,

and therefore

    BG ∥ CP.

Consequently, quadrilateral BPCG is a parallelogram.

Now let

    D = AP ∩ BC.

Since the diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other, D is the midpoint of BC.

Therefore AD is a median of △ABC.

Because G lies on AP, the point G belongs to the third median.

Hence the three medians of △ABC are concurrent.

---

## 3. Formalization Strategy

The objective of the Lean development was not merely to reproduce the final conclusion.

Instead, every mathematical step of Finlay's proof was formalized independently.

The resulting proof therefore mirrors the original argument almost line by line.

Rather than becoming a Lean script, the proof became a structured composition of reusable geometric theorems.

---

## 4. From Proof to Library

An unexpected consequence of the formalization was that several intermediate arguments naturally became independent library results.

Among them are:

- MidsegmentParallel
- ParallelogramParallelRecognition
- ParallelogramDiagonalMidpoint
- MidpointSymmetry

Although introduced solely to support Finlay's proof, these theorems immediately became reusable components for future developments.

Thus the geometry library was not designed a priori.

It emerged as a mathematical by-product of the proof itself.

---

## 5. Current Status

The first milestone of the project has therefore been reached.

The complete logical structure of Finlay's proof has been translated into Lean while preserving the sequence of the original geometric argument.

Only after achieving this faithful formalization does it become meaningful to ask a second question:

Which assumptions are genuinely primitive, and which can themselves be proved?

That investigation marks the beginning of the next stage of the project.
