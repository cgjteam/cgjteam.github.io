# Log 001 — Ian Finlay's Synthetic Proof

## Introduction

The concurrency of the three medians is one of the classical theorems of Euclidean geometry. Since antiquity, it has been proved using a wide variety of methods, including synthetic constructions, coordinate geometry, vector methods, affine geometry, and barycentric coordinates. The theorem also occupies an important place in the modern axiomatic treatment of geometry.

The purpose of the present project is **not** to establish a new proof of the theorem. Instead, the project investigates how a classical synthetic proof can be reconstructed, analyzed, documented, and ultimately prepared for formal verification in Lean.

In 2015, Ian Finlay presented a remarkably concise synthetic proof on Mathematics Stack Exchange. Rather than relying on coordinates or vectors, the argument is based on elementary geometric constructions involving midpoints, the Midsegment Theorem, and a parallelogram. This proof serves as the **initial case study** of the CGJteam Lab project.

## Theorem

**Centroid Theorem.**

The three medians of every non-degenerate triangle are concurrent. Furthermore, their common intersection point (the centroid $G$) divides each median in the ratio {% raw %}$2:1${% endraw %}, with the longer segment adjacent to the corresponding vertex.

## Ian Finlay's Construction

Let $AD$ and $BE$ be two medians of $\triangle ABC$, intersecting at point $G$. The first part of the argument establishes that $G$ divides both medians in the ratio {% raw %}$2:1${% endraw %}.

1. Extend the segment $AG$ beyond $G$ to a point $P$ such that $|AG| = |GP|$. Thus, $G$ is the midpoint of segment $AP$.

2. Using the Midsegment Theorem in triangles $\triangle ABP$ and $\triangle ACP$, it follows that $CG \parallel BP$ and $BG \parallel CP$.

3. Consequently, quadrilateral $BGCP$ is a parallelogram.

4. The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect one another. Therefore, the midpoint of $BC$ lies on the diagonal $GP$.

5. Since $A$, $G$, and $P$ are collinear, the same midpoint also lies on line $AG$.

6. Hence, the line through vertex $A$ and point $G$ passes through the midpoint of side $BC$. It is therefore the third median of $\triangle ABC$.

Since point $G$ already lies on the other two medians, all three medians are concurrent.
