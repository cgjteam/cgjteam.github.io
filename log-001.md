# Log 001 — Ian Finlay's Synthetic Proof

### Introduction
The concurrency of the three medians is one of the classical theorems of Euclidean geometry. Numerous proofs are known, ranging from coordinate and vector methods to affine and barycentric approaches. 

In 2015, Ian Finlay presented a remarkably concise synthetic proof on Mathematics Stack Exchange. Rather than relying on coordinates or vectors, the argument is based on elementary geometric constructions involving midpoints, the Midsegment Theorem, and a parallelogram. This proof serves as the starting point of the CGJteam Lab project. Our objective is not to replace the original argument but to analyze its logical structure, identify its hidden dependencies, and prepare it for formal verification.

### Theorem
The three medians of any non-degenerate triangle are concurrent. Furthermore, their common intersection point (the centroid $G$) divides each median in the ratio $2:1$, with the longer segment adjacent to the corresponding vertex.

### Ian Finlay's Construction
Let $AD$ and $BE$ be two medians of $\triangle ABC$, intersecting at point $G$. The first part of the argument establishes that $G$ divides both medians in the ratio $2:1$.

1. Extend the segment $AG$ beyond $G$ to a point $P$ such that $|AG| = |GP|$. Thus, $G$ is the midpoint of segment $AP$.
2. Using the Midsegment Theorem in triangles $\triangle ABP$ and $\triangle ACP$, it follows that $CG \parallel BP$ and $BG \parallel CP$.
3. Consequently, quadrilateral $BGCP$ is a parallelogram.



4. The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect one another. Therefore, the midpoint of $BC$ lies on the diagonal $GP$.
5. Since $A, G,$ and $P$ are collinear, the same midpoint also lies on line $AG$.
6. Hence, the line through vertex $A$ and point $G$ passes through the midpoint of side $BC$. It is therefore the third median of $\triangle ABC$.

Since point $G$ already lies on the other two medians, all three medians are concurrent.

### Sources
* Ian Finlay, *Proving that the medians of a triangle are concurrent*, Mathematics Stack Exchange, 2015.
