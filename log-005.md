# Log 005 — The Midsegment Theorem as the Engine of the Proof

## Introduction

The Midsegment Theorem is the first explicit geometric result used in Finlay's construction. Although it is one of the standard theorems of elementary Euclidean geometry, its role in this proof is fundamental. Every subsequent step depends on the parallelism established by this theorem.

## The Midsegment Theorem

Let $D$ and $E$ be the midpoints of two sides of a triangle $ABC$. Then

$$
DE \parallel AB
$$

and

$$
|DE|=\frac{1}{2}|AB|.
$$

The theorem provides both a parallelism relation and a metric relation.

## Why Is It Needed?

In Finlay's construction, the theorem is applied to the triangle whose sides contain the two selected medians. Its two conclusions play different logical roles:

1. **The parallelism**, namely $DE \parallel AB$, produces equal corresponding angles, allowing for the establishment of triangle similarity.

2. **The length relation**, namely $ |DE|=\frac{1}{2} |AB| $, determines the similarity ratio of the two triangles.

Neither conclusion alone is sufficient; both are required for the argument establishing the $2:1$ division of the medians.

## Logical Contribution

Within Finlay's proof, the Midsegment Theorem acts as the first non-trivial inference. It transforms midpoint information into geometric relations that can be propagated through similarity. Without this theorem, the proof cannot proceed.
