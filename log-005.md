# Log 005 — One Theorem Later

## Reflections after the First Complete Formalization

### Introduction

The original objective of the CGJteam Lab project was intentionally modest.

Rather than attempting to formalize a complete theory of synthetic geometry, we selected a single classical theorem: Ian Finlay's elegant proof of the concurrency of the three medians of a triangle. The goal was simple: understand the existing Lean geometry library, complete one substantial formal proof, and document the entire process.

This objective has now been achieved.

Looking back, however, the completed proof turned out to be far less interesting than the questions that appeared along the way.

The purpose of this log is therefore not to describe another theorem. Instead, it summarizes what we learned after completing our first full-scale formalization and after examining several important projects related to formal geometry.

---

## From Proof to Questions

At the beginning of the project our attention was focused almost entirely on one problem:

> Can this classical proof be formalized in Lean 4?

After several days of work the answer became positive.

Unexpectedly, however, finishing the proof did not produce a feeling of completion. Instead, it revealed a new collection of questions that had been invisible at the beginning.

The formal proof was correct.

The Lean code compiled.

Yet understanding the resulting development proved surprisingly difficult.

The project now contained numerous auxiliary definitions, technical lemmas, accessor theorems, and dependencies accumulated during the construction process. Every element had been introduced for a reason, but after the proof had been completed it became increasingly difficult to distinguish between mathematical necessity and implementation history.

This was an unexpected observation.

Writing a formally verified proof turned out to be only the first stage.

Understanding the architecture of that proof became a second problem altogether.

---

## Looking Beyond the Project

The questions raised by the Finlay formalization naturally led us to a broader investigation.

If these difficulties appeared after completing a single classical theorem, how have other researchers approached the same problem?

Rather than immediately extending our own library, we spent some time studying several influential formalization projects and the ideas behind them.

One of the first observations was that there is no single objective shared by all formal geometry projects.

Each project is motivated by a different question.

Hilbert's *Foundations of Geometry* reminded us that geometry can be viewed primarily as an exercise in organizing mathematical knowledge. Primitive notions, independence of axioms, consistency and logical structure become central questions. The goal is not efficient computation but the architecture of the theory itself.

Tarski approached the same subject from a different direction. His geometry is intentionally minimalistic. By reducing the entire theory to points together with only two primitive relations—betweenness and congruence—he demonstrated how surprisingly rich a mathematical theory can emerge from a very small logical core. Simplicity, decidability and metamathematical properties became the guiding principles.

Early Lean developments demonstrated that these classical axiomatic systems could indeed be implemented inside a modern proof assistant. They also revealed the practical cost of working directly at the foundational level. Building the foundations proved possible; building large reusable libraries on top of them proved considerably more demanding.

LeanGeo represents a noticeable shift in philosophy. Rather than searching for the smallest possible axiomatic basis, it focuses on constructing a practical environment for formal reasoning. High-level geometric theorems replace many low-level derivations, while routine logical reasoning is delegated to SMT solvers. At the same time, the project explicitly serves as a benchmark for AI-assisted theorem proving.

Terence Tao's experience illustrates yet another perspective. Formalization is not pursued for its own sake but as a practical instrument for verifying sophisticated mathematics, coordinating collaborative projects and increasing confidence in long and technically demanding proofs.

These projects differ substantially in both motivation and methodology.

Some pursue elegant foundations.

Others pursue practical theorem libraries.

Others focus on collaboration or artificial intelligence.

None of these objectives is incompatible with the others.

They simply answer different questions.

---

## Our Own Perspective

The survey of existing projects did not provide a direct answer to the questions that emerged during our own experiment.

This was not surprising.

After all, we had completed only a single theorem.

Nevertheless, that single theorem was sufficient to change our perspective.

At the beginning of the project we believed that the principal challenge would be to construct a formally verified proof.

After completing the development we began to suspect that this is only the first step.

The second challenge is understanding the resulting formal theory.

Which definitions are fundamental?

Which lemmas represent genuine mathematics?

Which ones merely support the proof assistant?

Which dependencies are essential, and which are historical artifacts accumulated during the construction process?

These questions are difficult to answer even for the author of the formalization.

Completing a proof therefore does not conclude the work.

In many respects, it marks the beginning of a different stage: understanding, maintaining and gradually improving an evolving formal mathematical library.

This observation does not contradict the goals of projects such as LeanGeo or the work of Terence Tao.

Rather, it complements them.

Verification is indispensable.

Library design is indispensable.

Collaboration is indispensable.

Yet, after completing our first experiment, we became increasingly interested in another question:

How should a mature formal mathematical library be understood after it has already been built?

---

## An Unplanned Investigation

None of the literature discussed in this log was part of the original project plan.

Our intention was simply to complete one formal proof.

The broader investigation began only after the formalization had been finished.

Questions about Hilbert, Tarski, LeanGeo, Terence Tao and other formalization projects emerged naturally as we tried to understand our own experience.

In retrospect, this unplanned survey became one of the most valuable parts of the project.

It placed a relatively small Lean development into a much broader context and helped us understand that different research groups pursue remarkably different objectives.

Perhaps the most important lesson was that formalization itself is not a single discipline.

Depending on the perspective, it may be viewed as an exercise in mathematical foundations, software engineering, automated reasoning, artificial intelligence or mathematical collaboration.

Recognizing these different perspectives has become an important outcome of our first completed project.
