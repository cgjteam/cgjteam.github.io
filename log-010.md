# Log 010 - From Formal Proof to Geometry Atlas

## From a Single Formal Proof to a Structured Exploration of Geometry

### Introduction

The first phase of CGJteam Lab began with a deliberately limited
objective.

The goal was not to build a complete formal geometry library.

The goal was to understand and verify one classical synthetic proof.

The chosen example was Ian Finlay's proof of the concurrency of the
medians of a triangle.

The formalization has now been completed.

However, the result of this experiment was not only a verified theorem.

The process revealed a possible methodology for studying geometry
itself.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## What the First Experiment Produced

The completed Finlay formalization produced several distinct outcomes.

First, it produced a formal theorem:

Finlay

The theorem verifies the classical result that the three medians of a
triangle are concurrent.

Second, it produced reusable mathematical components:

-   midpoint relations,
-   collinearity transformations,
-   midsegment arguments,
-   parallelogram constructions,
-   diagonal midpoint properties.

Third, it produced information about dependencies.

The proof revealed which concepts were essential and which belonged only
to the surrounding geometric intuition.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## From Proofs to Mathematical Objects

A traditional mathematical text is usually organized around theorems.

A formal library requires another level of organization.

## The Geometry Atlas Idea

The next stage of CGJteam Lab is not simply the accumulation of more
theorems.

The objective is to create a structured atlas of geometric concepts.

Such an atlas should record:

-   the primitive notions required,
-   equivalent descriptions of concepts,
-   transformations preserving them,
-   theorems depending on them,
-   different mathematical perspectives from which they can be
    understood.

A single geometric object may appear differently depending on the
viewpoint.

For example, a midpoint can be studied as:

-   a synthetic incidence relation,
-   a metric equality condition,
-   an affine invariant,
-   a fixed point of a transformation.

Formalization makes these connections explicit.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Geometry as Layers of Structure

The first experiment suggests that geometry naturally separates into
layers.

### Incidence Layer

The most primitive level contains:

-   points,
-   collinearity,
-   intersection relations.

### Affine Layer

The next level contains:

-   parallelism,
-   midpoint structures,
-   parallelograms,
-   affine transformations.

### Metric Layer

A richer layer introduces:

-   distances,
-   congruence,
-   angles,
-   circles.

### Transformation Layer

A further viewpoint studies:

-   invariants,
-   symmetry groups,
-   Klein's Erlangen program.

The Finlay proof occupies mainly the incidence and affine layers.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Formalization as Mathematical Archaeology

The process of formalization can be viewed as a form of mathematical
archaeology.

A classical proof is like an artifact.

It contains visible arguments and hidden assumptions.

Formalization carefully removes the surrounding context and exposes the
underlying structure.

The result is not only a machine-checked proof.

It is a reconstruction of the mathematical mechanism itself.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Role of Lean

Lean is not merely a verification tool in this project.

It acts as an instrument of analysis.

The proof assistant forces explicit answers to questions that informal
mathematics often leaves implicit:

-   What is the definition of the object?
-   Which assumptions are actually used?
-   Which theorem provides the transition?
-   Which concepts belong to the theory and which belong only to the
    proof?

These questions guide the development of the atlas.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Conclusion

The first CGJteam Lab experiment started with one theorem.

It ended with a broader question:

How can formalization help us understand the internal organization of
mathematics?

The answer is not only through verification.

Formalization reveals structures, dependencies and relationships that
remain hidden in ordinary presentation.

The next stage is therefore not the construction of a larger collection
of proofs.

It is the exploration of geometry as a structured mathematical
landscape.
