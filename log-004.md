# From Finlay's Proof to a Reusable Geometry Library

**Building the first reusable mathematical theories extracted from the formal verification of Finlay's proof.**

**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** Library Development

---

## 1. Overview

The previous stage introduced the first reusable theorems extracted from the formal verification of Finlay's proof.

The next objective was different.

Rather than encapsulating complete arguments, we began constructing a reusable geometry library around them.

This required identifying pieces of mathematical knowledge that appeared repeatedly in independent proofs and promoting them to permanent components of the library.

The present log documents the first stage of this transition.

---

## 2. Building a Geometry API

The first reusable components were not deep geometric theorems.

Instead, they were elementary facts that repeatedly interrupted larger synthetic arguments.

Although mathematically simple, they appeared so frequently that proving them again and again obscured the essential ideas of the proofs.

The first example is

<pre class="formal">
MidpointSymmetry A B M
</pre>

The theorem concerns the segment `AB` together with its midpoint `M`.

Geometrically, it states that if `M` is the midpoint of `AB`, then it is equally the midpoint of `BA`.

This elementary symmetry occurs throughout synthetic geometry.

Once encapsulated, later proofs no longer need to reconstruct this trivial argument.

---

The same phenomenon appeared for parallelism.

The theorem

<pre class="formal">
ParallelSymmetry A B C D
</pre>

concerns two parallel lines determined by the points `A`, `B`, `C` and `D`.

Its mathematical content is simply

> `AB ∥ CD`  if and only if  `CD ∥ AB`.

Although obvious geometrically, this symmetry was repeatedly required during formal proofs.

Encapsulating it considerably simplified subsequent developments.

---

Synthetic proofs also require changing the orientation of individual segments.

The theorem

<pre class="formal">
ParallelSwapFirstLine A B C D
</pre>

states that

> `AB ∥ CD`

implies

> `BA ∥ CD`.

Its companion theorems

<pre class="formal">
ParallelSwapSecondLine A B C D
ParallelSwapBoth A B C D
</pre>

provide the analogous transformations for the second supporting line and for both lines simultaneously.

Together these elementary results form the first reusable interface for routine manipulations involving parallelism.

---

A similar situation occurs whenever one endpoint of a parallel segment is replaced by another point on the same supporting line.

The theorem

<pre class="formal">
collinear_parallel_substitute A B C D E
</pre>

expresses the following geometric principle.

If

- `AB ∥ CD`, and
- the point `E` is collinear with `A` and `B`,

then the parallelism may be transferred to the segment determined by `E`.

Its symmetric companion

<pre class="formal">
collinear_parallel_substitute2 A B C D E
</pre>

performs the analogous substitution on the opposite supporting line.

These two theorems eliminate one of the most common routine transformations appearing in synthetic geometry.

---

## 3. Building Accessor Theorems

The next class of reusable results arose from compound geometric definitions.

Many geometric objects are introduced by conjunctions describing several independent properties.

During formal proofs, however, one usually needs only a single component of such a definition.

Repeatedly decomposing conjunctions soon became one of the most common sources of routine code.

This observation led to the introduction of dedicated accessor theorems.

The first example is

<pre class="formal">
MedianIntersectionCollinearCGF A B C E F G
</pre>

The theorem concerns the intersection point `G` of two medians in the triangle `ABC`.

Its geometric content is simply that

- the points `C`, `G` and `F`

are collinear.

Instead of repeatedly unpacking the definition of a median intersection, later proofs obtain this fact directly from the theorem.

---

Its companion theorem

<pre class="formal">
MedianIntersectionCollinearBGE A B C E F G
</pre>

extracts the second collinearity relation contained in the same definition.

Geometrically, it states that

- the points `B`, `G` and `E`

are collinear.

Together, the two theorems provide immediate access to the defining geometric structure of the median intersection.

---

The same idea was subsequently applied to segment intersections.

The theorem

<pre class="formal">
SegmentIntersectionCollinearADP A B C D P
</pre>

states that

- the points `A`, `D` and `P`

are collinear.

Its companion

<pre class="formal">
SegmentIntersectionCollinearBDC A B C D P
</pre>

states that

- the points `B`, `D` and `C`

are collinear.

Again, neither theorem introduces new geometry.

Their purpose is to expose individual geometric consequences of a compound definition through a simple reusable interface.

Taken together, these accessor theorems considerably shorten later formal proofs while making their mathematical structure significantly clearer.

---

## 4. Building the Theory of Parallelograms

Once the elementary API had been established, attention shifted to a more substantial mathematical object.

The previous stage had already shown that parallelograms appeared naturally in several independent synthetic arguments.

At this point they ceased to be merely auxiliary constructions.

Instead, their fundamental properties began to form the nucleus of an independent mathematical theory.

The first step was the theorem

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramParallelCharacterization A B C D
</pre>

The theorem concerns the quadrilateral `ABCD`.

Geometrically, it identifies a parallelogram through the conditions

- `AB ∥ CD`,
- `BC ∥ AD`.

This characterization became the natural entry point to every subsequent argument involving parallelograms.

---

The next reusable theorem was

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramOppositeSidesCongruent A B C D
</pre>

Again the theorem concerns the quadrilateral `ABCD`.

Its mathematical content is one of the classical properties of parallelograms:

- `AB ≅ CD`,
- `BC ≅ AD`.

Instead of proving these congruences whenever they were needed, they became available as a direct consequence of the parallelogram structure.

This marked the first separation between the construction of a parallelogram and the independent use of its properties.

---

Another essential component was introduced as

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramDiagonalsBisect A B C D
</pre>

The theorem again refers to the quadrilateral `ABCD`.

Geometrically, it states that

- the diagonals `AC` and `BD` bisect one another.

This property originally appeared inside the formal verification of `FinlayProof`, where it was needed to establish a midpoint.

After encapsulation it became an independent theorem that could be reused without any reference to its historical origin.

---

The converse direction was represented by

<pre class="formal">
OnePairParallelCongruentRecognition A B C D
</pre>

The theorem concerns the quadrilateral `ABCD`.

Its geometric statement is the familiar recognition criterion

- `AB ∥ CD`,
- `AB ≅ CD`

imply

- `ABCD` is a parallelogram.

This theorem completed the first collection of reusable characterizations describing parallelograms from different mathematical viewpoints.

Although introduced during the formal verification of Finlay's proof, these theorems already belong to an independent mathematical theory.

---

## 5. Transitional Results

Not every theorem extracted from the development immediately found its final place inside the emerging library.

Some still reflected the historical order in which they had been discovered.

One example is

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramParallelRecognition A B C D
</pre>

The theorem concerns the quadrilateral `ABCD`.

Geometrically, it recognizes a parallelogram from the two pairs of opposite parallel sides constructed during the formalization.

Although mathematically correct, its current name still records its historical origin rather than its mathematical role.

---

A similar example is

<pre class="formal">
FinlayParallelogramStep A B C D
</pre>

The theorem concerns the same auxiliary parallelogram.

Its mathematical purpose is to derive the midpoint required in the final part of the proof from the diagonal property of a parallelogram.

Like the previous theorem, it represents an intermediate stage in the evolution of the library.

Both theorems are expected to migrate into the general theory of parallelograms once that theory becomes sufficiently complete.

---

## 6. Naming Policy

The transition from a single formal proof to a reusable geometry library also required a gradual revision of the naming policy.

Early names naturally reflected the historical order in which the corresponding theorems had been discovered.

As the library matured, however, theorem names increasingly described the mathematical object to which they belonged rather than the proof from which they originated.

This tendency can already be observed throughout the present development.

For example,

<pre class="formal">
MidpointSymmetry A B M
</pre>

belongs naturally to the theory of midpoints,

while

<pre class="formal">
ParallelSymmetry A B C D
</pre>

and its companion theorems belong to the elementary theory of parallelism.

Similarly,

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramParallelCharacterization A B C D
</pre>

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramOppositeSidesCongruent A B C D
</pre>

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramDiagonalsBisect A B C D
</pre>

are named according to the mathematical properties they describe, rather than according to the proofs in which they first appeared.

Only a small number of transitional results, such as

<pre class="formal">
FinlayParallelogramStep A B C D
</pre>

still preserve their historical origin in the theorem name.

These names document the evolution of the project and are expected to disappear naturally as the corresponding mathematical theories become complete.

---

## 7. Outlook

The developments recorded in this log represent the first stage in the transformation of a formal proof into a reusable mathematical library.

The initial effort focused on identifying recurring mathematical ideas, eliminating routine reasoning, and exposing frequently used geometric facts through stable theorem interfaces.

At the same time, elementary helper theorems, accessor theorems and the first characterizations of parallelograms gradually separated the developing library from the original structure of Finlay's proof.

The resulting collection of reusable components is no longer tied to a single formal verification.

Instead, it forms the foundation for subsequent developments in synthetic geometry.

The next stage of the project will build complete mathematical theories on top of these components, beginning with a systematic formal treatment of parallelograms.
