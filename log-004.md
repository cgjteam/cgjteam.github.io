# First Geometry API Extracted from Finlay

**How formal verification revealed the first reusable interfaces of a synthetic geometry library.**

**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** Library Development

---

## 1. Overview

The previous stages focused on the formal verification of Ian Finlay's synthetic proof and the extraction of its internal logical structure.

The first milestone showed that a classical geometric argument could be decomposed into independent formal steps:

- construction of parallel relations,
- recognition of a parallelogram,
- analysis of diagonal intersections,
- identification of a midpoint,
- construction of the third median.

The next question concerned the transition from a single proof to a reusable library.

Which mathematical facts should become permanent interfaces, and which should remain internal details of a particular argument?

The answer began with the identification of elementary transformations that repeatedly appeared during the formal development.

---

## 2. From Formal Proofs to Reusable Interfaces

A formal proof often contains many small geometric transformations.

Although these transformations are mathematically elementary, they occur frequently enough that repeating them inside every proof obscures the main argument.

The first reusable components extracted from Finlay's proof were therefore not advanced geometric theorems.

They were elementary structural operations:

- changing the order of points on a line,
- changing the orientation of a parallel relation,
- transferring a known relation through a collinearity condition.

These operations form the first layer of a geometry API.

---

## 3. Elementary Collinearity Transformations

The first example is the symmetry of collinearity.

The theorem

```lean
collinear_swap
```

expresses the fact that if three points are collinear, the last two points may be exchanged.

Geometrically:

\[
A,B,C \text{ collinear}
\]

implies

\[
A,C,B \text{ collinear}.
\]

Although elementary, this transformation appears repeatedly whenever the orientation of a synthetic argument changes.

In Finlay's proof it is used to convert the assumptions

\[
C,G,F
\]

and

\[
B,G,E
\]

into the orientations required by later lemmas.

---

## 4. Elementary Parallel Transformations

Parallelism requires similar basic operations.

The theorem

```lean
ParallelSymm
```

formalizes the symmetry of parallel lines.

It expresses:

\[
AB \parallel CD
\]

implies

\[
CD \parallel AB.
\]

The theorem

```lean
ParallelFlipRight
```

changes the orientation of the second line:

\[
AB \parallel CD
\]

implies

\[
AB \parallel DC.
\]

Together, these operations provide the elementary manipulation rules needed by later synthetic arguments.

For example, in Finlay's proof they are used to transform the parallel relations obtained from the Midsegment Theorem into the form required for constructing the parallelogram.

---

## 5. Synthetic Mechanisms Extracted from Finlay

Beyond elementary transformations, the formalization revealed larger reusable mechanisms.

The first important example is:

```lean
MidsegmentTheorem
```

This theorem captures the classical Midsegment Theorem:

if two points are midpoints of two sides of a triangle, then the segment joining them is parallel to the third side.

In Finlay's proof, this theorem is applied twice:

- once in triangle `ABP`,
- once in triangle `ACP`.

These two applications produce the two parallel relations needed to construct the auxiliary parallelogram.

---

A second reusable mechanism is:

```lean
ParallelCollinearLeft
```

This theorem expresses a common synthetic operation:

a parallel relation may be transferred from one point of a line to another point lying on the same line.

This corresponds exactly to the geometric step used in Finlay's proof:

from

\[
FG \parallel BP
\]

and

\[
C,F,G \text{ collinear}
\]

we obtain

\[
CG \parallel BP.
\]

Encapsulating this operation removes a frequent source of repetitive reasoning.

---

## 6. The First Parallelogram Interface

The formalization of Finlay's proof also showed that parallelograms form a natural next layer of the library.

At the current stage, the library does not yet contain a complete theory of parallelograms.

Instead, it contains the first structural interfaces required by the proof.

The theorem

```lean
ParallelogramOfParallel
```

formalizes the recognition step:

two pairs of opposite parallel sides determine a parallelogram.

The theorem

```lean
ParallelogramDiagonals
```

captures the diagonal property used in the final stage of Finlay's argument:

the diagonals of a parallelogram determine a midpoint relation.

These results represent the first extracted components of a future parallelogram theory.

---

## 7. Current Library State

The current development should be viewed as a transition stage.

The library now contains:

- basic geometric predicates,
- elementary transformations,
- reusable synthetic lemmas,
- first parallelogram mechanisms,
- the complete formal proof of Finlay's construction.

However, it does not yet constitute a complete synthetic geometry framework.

The current goal is not to maximize the number of available theorems.

The goal is to identify the smallest meaningful mathematical interfaces that allow classical synthetic arguments to be expressed clearly and modularly.

---

## 8. Outlook

The next stages of the project will continue the same process:

1. Formalize classical synthetic arguments.
2. Identify recurring mathematical patterns.
3. Extract reusable interfaces.
4. Develop independent geometric theories.

The first candidate for deeper development is the theory of parallelograms, followed by broader structures appearing naturally in triangle geometry.

The long-term objective is a reusable formal library where mathematical theories emerge from verified synthetic reasoning.
