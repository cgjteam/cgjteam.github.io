# Encapsulating Synthetic Arguments

**The first reusable components extracted during the formal verification of Finlay's proof.**

**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** Library Development

---

## 1. Overview

The formal verification of Finlay's proof produced considerably more than a verified theorem.

Several geometric arguments occurring repeatedly during the development were extracted from the proof and promoted to independent components of the geometry library.

This log documents the first stage of that process.

Rather than describing the proof itself, it records the mathematical objects that emerged from it.

---

## 2. Reusable Synthetic Arguments

The first complete geometric argument extracted from the proof was the Midsegment Theorem.

Instead of reconstructing its synthetic proof whenever it was needed, the entire argument was encapsulated as the theorem `MidsegmentParallel`.

```lean
have hFGparallelBP :=
  MidsegmentParallel
    B P A F G
    hFB hGP
```

The theorem represents not only the statement that the segment joining two midpoints is parallel to the third side of a triangle.

It also encapsulates the complete synthetic argument proving this fact.

This was the first theorem in the project designed primarily for reuse rather than for a single application inside Finlay's proof.

---

## 3. Helper Lemmas for Synthetic Geometry

The next group of additions consists of elementary geometric transformations that appear repeatedly in synthetic arguments.

| Theorem | Mathematical role |
|---------|-------------------|
| `MidpointSymmetry` | If M is the midpoint of AB, then M is also the midpoint of BA. |
| `ParallelSymmetry` | Exchanges the two parallel lines. |
| `ParallelSwapFirstLine` | Reverses the orientation of the first parallel line. |
| `ParallelSwapSecondLine` | Reverses the orientation of the second parallel line. |
| `ParallelSwapBoth` | Reverses the orientation of both parallel lines simultaneously. |
| `collinear_parallel_substitute` | Replaces one endpoint of a parallel segment by a collinear point. |
| `collinear_parallel_substitute2` | Symmetric version of the previous lemma. |

These theorems introduce almost no new mathematics.

Their purpose is to eliminate repetitive manipulations that naturally occur in synthetic geometry, making later proofs significantly shorter and easier to read.

---

## 4. Accessor Lemmas

Several compound geometric definitions contain information that is repeatedly extracted during formal proofs.

Instead of repeatedly decomposing conjunctions, dedicated accessor lemmas were introduced.

| Theorem | Extracted information |
|---------|-----------------------|
| `MedianIntersectionCollinearCGF` | Points C, G and F are collinear. |
| `MedianIntersectionCollinearBGE` | Points B, G and E are collinear. |
| `SegmentIntersectionCollinearADP` | Points A, D and P are collinear. |
| `SegmentIntersectionCollinearBDC` | Points B, D and C are collinear. |

These lemmas do not establish new geometric facts.

They provide a clean mathematical interface to compound definitions and considerably simplify subsequent proofs.

## 5. Characterizing Parallelograms

The formalization of Finlay's proof required the first systematic organization of the theory of parallelograms.

Rather than treating a parallelogram as a single primitive notion, the library gradually introduced several complementary characterizations describing its fundamental geometric properties.

| Theorem | Mathematical role |
|---------|-------------------|
| `ParallelogramParallelCharacterization` | A parallelogram is characterized by two pairs of opposite parallel sides. |
| `ParallelogramCongruentCharacterizationPrim` | Opposite sides of a parallelogram are congruent. |
| `ParallelogramDiagonalCharacterizationPrim` | The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect one another. |
| `OnePairParallelCongruentRecognition` | Recognition theorem: one pair of opposite sides being both parallel and congruent is sufficient to recognize a parallelogram. |

These theorems represent the beginning of an independent theory of parallelograms.

Although introduced while verifying Finlay's proof, they are no longer tied to that proof and are intended for general use throughout the geometry library.

---

## 6. Transitional Theorems

Not every theorem introduced during development has yet reached its final form.

One example is

```lean
theorem FinlayParallelogramStep ...
```

whose purpose is to derive the midpoint of one diagonal from the parallelogram construction used in Finlay's argument.

Although mathematically correct, the current name still reflects the historical origin of the theorem rather than its mathematical content.

This theorem will eventually become part of the general theory of parallelograms and will most likely be renamed accordingly.

Its presence documents an intermediate stage in the evolution of the library.

---

## 7. Naming Policy

The development described in this log naturally led to a revision of the naming policy.

Whenever possible, theorem names are now chosen according to the mathematical theory to which they belong rather than the proof in which they were first discovered.

This principle can already be observed throughout the library.

For example,

- `MidsegmentParallel` belongs to the theory of triangle midsegments,
- `MidpointSymmetry` belongs to the theory of midpoints,
- `ParallelSymmetry` belongs to the theory of parallelism,
- characterization theorems belong to the theory of parallelograms.

The long-term objective is for the library structure to mirror the organization of synthetic geometry itself.

---

## 8. Outlook

The first version of the geometry library now contains considerably more than the formal verification of Finlay's proof.

It already includes reusable synthetic arguments, helper lemmas, accessor lemmas and the first characterization theorems for parallelograms.

Future development will continue extending these mathematical components into independent theories while reducing the amount of proof-specific code.
