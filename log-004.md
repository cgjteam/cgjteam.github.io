# Log 004 — The First Hidden Assumption: Do Two Medians Always Intersect?

## Introduction

The first explicit step in Finlay's construction is to consider the intersection point of two medians. At first glance, this appears to be an elementary observation. However, from the perspective of logical analysis, the existence of this point is not part of the theorem itself; it is a prerequisite for the construction.

This raises a natural question:

**Why is the intersection point well-defined?**

## The Hidden Assumption

The proof begins with

> Let \(G = AD \cap BE\).

This notation implicitly assumes that the medians \(AD\) and \(BE\) intersect at a unique point. In classical geometry, this fact is usually taken for granted. For formal verification, however, every existence statement requires justification.

## Logical Dependency

The existence of \(G\) follows if the two medians are not parallel. Thus, the proof depends on the following auxiliary statement:

**Auxiliary Statement:** Two distinct medians of a non-degenerate triangle are not parallel.

Only after establishing this fact is the definition \(G = AD \cap BE\) logically justified.

## Research Question

A vector proof establishes the auxiliary statement independently of the concurrency theorem. At present, no independent synthetic proof has been identified within the scope of this project. Whether such a proof exists in the classical literature remains to be investigated.

This observation illustrates the purpose of the project: rather than accepting every construction step as self-evident, we ask whether each prerequisite is genuinely independent of the theorem being proved. This distinction becomes particularly important when preparing the proof for formal verification.
