# From Finlay's Proof to the Theory of Parallelograms

**Date:** 2026-07-11  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** First Milestone

---

## 1. Overview

The original objective of the CGJteam Lab project was intentionally modest: to produce a faithful formal verification of Ian Finlay's synthetic proof that the three medians of a triangle are concurrent.

Rather than designing a geometry library in advance, the development started from a single classical proof. During the formalization it became apparent that the parallelogram arguments occurring in Finlay's proof form a coherent local theory with a characteristic pattern of use.

The proof does not merely invoke isolated lemmas about parallelograms. Instead, it repeatedly follows the same sequence:

1. construct an appropriate geometric configuration,
2. recognize it as a parallelogram,
3. apply a theorem from the local theory of parallelograms,
4. derive the geometric consequence required by the original proof.

This observation naturally led to the extraction of an independent **Theory of Parallelograms**, together with a reusable protocol describing how this local theory is activated within formal proofs.

Let △ABC be a triangle.

Let E and F be the midpoints of AC and AB, respectively, and let

  G = BE ∩ CF.

The objective is to prove that G also lies on the third median of △ABC.

Finlay's key observation is that the original problem can be transformed into a statement about a parallelogram.

To achieve this, he introduces an auxiliary point P so that G is the midpoint of AP.

## 3. Finlay's Five-Step Argument

### Step 1 — Construction of Parallel Relations

Since F and G are the midpoints of AB and AP in △ABP,

  FG ∥ BP.

Because C, F and G are collinear,

  CG ∥ BP.

Similarly, since E and G are the midpoints of AC and AP in △ACP,

  EG ∥ CP,

and therefore

  BG ∥ CP.

This step belongs entirely to the theory of midsegments and parallel lines.

From the perspective of the formal proof, its role is to construct the parallel relations required for recognizing a parallelogram.

---

### Step 2 — Recognition of a Parallelogram

Since

  CG ∥ BP

and

  BG ∥ CP,

quadrilateral BPCG is a parallelogram.

This is the recognition phase of the proof. Once the configuration has been identified as a parallelogram, the local Theory of Parallelograms becomes available.

---

### Step 3 — Construction of the Diagonal Configuration

Let

  D = AP ∩ BC.

Since AP and BC are the diagonals of parallelogram BPCG, the geometric configuration required for the diagonal theorem is now complete.

This step prepares the application of the local theory but does not yet use any specific property of parallelograms.

---

### Step 4 — Application of the Diagonal Property

The proof now invokes one of the fundamental theorems of the Theory of Parallelograms:

> The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other.

Applying this theorem immediately yields that D is the midpoint of BC.

Therefore AD is a median of △ABC.

This is the key application of the local theory.

---

### Step 5 — Geometric Consequence

Because G lies on AP and AP coincides with the median AD,

the point G belongs to the third median.

Consequently, the three medians of △ABC are concurrent.

At this stage the proof leaves the local Theory of Parallelograms and returns to the original centroid theorem.

## 4. From Proof to Theory

Although Finlay's proof is presented as a sequence of elementary geometric arguments, the Lean formalization reveals an additional structural layer.

The proof repeatedly follows the same protocol for using a local geometric theory.

| Phase of the proof | Role |
| :--- | :--- |
| Construction | Build the geometric configuration required for a local theory |
| Recognition | Identify the configuration as an instance of a known object |
| Property application | Invoke a theorem from the local theory |
| Geometric consequence | Continue the original proof using the obtained result |

For Finlay's proof, the local theory is the Theory of Parallelograms.

This protocol corresponds closely to the structure of the formal Lean proof, where recognition of a parallelogram is followed by the application of the diagonal theorem and the derivation of the required midpoint property.

## 5. Impact on the Geometry Library

The most important consequence of the formalization was not merely the identification of reusable parallelogram theorems.

More significantly, the formal proof revealed a reusable pattern for applying a local geometric theory.

The Theory of Parallelograms therefore consists of two complementary components:

- mathematical knowledge:
  - definitions,
  - characterization theorems,
  - properties of opposite sides,
  - properties of diagonals,
  - midpoint results;

- protocol of use:
  - construction,
  - recognition,
  - property application,
  - geometric consequence.

This viewpoint changes the role of formalization within the project.

Rather than verifying isolated theorems, formalization becomes a method for discovering reusable mathematical theories together with recurring reasoning patterns describing how these theories are used.

---

## 6. Towards a Theory of Parallelograms

The next stage of the project is no longer concerned solely with the centroid theorem.

Instead, the objective is to develop an independent **Theory of Parallelograms** as a reusable component of the Geometry Library.

This development has two complementary objectives.

The first is mathematical:

- characterization of parallelograms,
- properties of opposite sides,
- diagonal theorems,
- midpoint properties,
- congruence results,
- reusable synthetic constructions.

The second is methodological:

to describe how the Theory of Parallelograms is activated and used inside formal proofs.

The current formalization suggests the following general protocol:

1. construct a suitable geometric configuration;
2. recognize the configuration as a parallelogram;
3. apply a theorem from the Theory of Parallelograms;
4. derive the geometric consequence required by the surrounding proof.

This protocol is expected to reappear in many synthetic proofs beyond Finlay's theorem.

The formal Lean development therefore shows that reusable mathematical knowledge consists not only of definitions and theorems, but also of recurring patterns describing how local theories are activated and applied within formal proofs.
