# Finlay's Synthetic Proof

**Date:** 2026-07-11  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** First Milestone

---

## 1. Overview

The original objective of the CGJteam Lab project was intentionally modest: to produce a faithful formal verification of Ian Finlay's synthetic proof that the three medians of a triangle are concurrent.

Rather than designing a geometry library in advance, the development started from a single classical proof. During the formalization it became apparent that several parts of the proof form independent geometric theories. These theories were gradually extracted as reusable modules of the Geometry Library.

Thus the library emerged naturally from the proof itself rather than preceding it.

Let △ABC be a triangle.

Let E and F be the midpoints of AC and AB, respectively, and let

  G = BE ∩ CF.

The objective is to prove that G also lies on the third median of △ABC.

Finlay's key observation is that the original problem can be transformed into a statement about a parallelogram.

To achieve this, he introduces an auxiliary point P so that G is the midpoint of AP.

The remainder of the proof consists of five elementary geometric steps.

## 3. Finlay's Five-Step Argument

### Step 1 — Application of the Midsegment Theorem

Since F and G are the midpoints of AB and AP in △ABP,

  FG ∥ BP.

Because C, F and G are collinear,

  CG ∥ BP.

Similarly, since E and G are the midpoints of AC and AP in △ACP,

  EG ∥ CP,

and therefore

  BG ∥ CP.

This step belongs entirely to the theory of midsegments and parallel lines.

---

### Step 2 — Recognition of a Parallelogram

Since

  CG ∥ BP

and

  BG ∥ CP,

quadrilateral BPCG is a parallelogram.

At this point the proof leaves triangle geometry and enters the independent theory of parallelograms.

---

### Step 3 — The Diagonal Intersection

Let

  D = AP ∩ BC.

Since AP and BC are the diagonals of parallelogram BPCG, the point D is their intersection.

No further properties of the original triangle are required.

---

### Step 4 — Diagonals of a Parallelogram

A fundamental theorem of parallelogram theory states that the diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other.

Hence D is the midpoint of BC.

Therefore AD is a median of △ABC.

This theorem is completely independent of the centroid problem and forms a reusable component of the Geometry Library.

---

### Step 5 — Conclusion

Because G lies on AP and AP coincides with the median AD,

the point G belongs to the third median.

Consequently, the three medians of △ABC are concurrent.

---

## 4. Mathematical Structure

Although the proof is presented as five elementary steps, its mathematical structure is better understood as a sequence of transitions between independent geometric theories.

| Stage | Geometric theory |
| :--- | :--- |
| Midsegment theorem | Triangle geometry |
| Parallel lines | Parallel theory |
| Recognition of a parallelogram | Parallelogram theory |
| Diagonals bisect each other | Parallelogram theory |
| Midpoint implies median | Median theory |

Viewed in this way, Finlay's proof is not merely a proof of a single theorem. It is a composition of several independent geometric theories connected by a carefully chosen auxiliary construction.

This observation became one of the guiding principles of the Geometry Library. Instead of organizing the library around individual proofs, reusable mathematical theories are extracted whenever they appear naturally during formalization.

---

## 5. Impact on the Geometry Library

The formal verification of Finlay's proof led to much more than a certified proof of the centroid theorem.

It revealed several reusable mathematical components, including

- midpoint theory,
- parallel line arguments,
- parallelogram theory,
- reusable synthetic proof patterns.

The subsequent architectural refactoring of the Geometry Library followed the same philosophy. Primitive notions were replaced by mathematically justified definitions whenever possible, and elementary geometric facts became ordinary Lean theorems rather than independent axioms.

Finlay's proof therefore served not only as the first formal result of the project, but also as the prototype for the methodology adopted throughout the Geometry Library.
