# From Finlay's Proof to Parallelogram Reasoning

**Date:** 2026-07-11  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** First Milestone

---

## 1. Overview

The original objective of the CGJteam Lab project was intentionally modest: to produce a faithful formal verification of Ian Finlay's synthetic proof that the three medians of a triangle are concurrent.

Rather than designing a geometry library in advance, the development started from a single classical proof. During the formalization it became apparent that the parallelogram arguments occurring in Finlay's proof form a coherent and reusable part of the development with a characteristic pattern of use.

The proof does not merely invoke isolated lemmas about parallelograms. Instead, it repeatedly follows the same sequence:

1. construct the parallel relations,
2. recognize a parallelogram,
3. construct the diagonal configuration,
4. apply the parallelogram diagonal theorem,
5. derive the geometric consequence required by the original proof.

This observation naturally led to the extraction of a reusable parallelogram component together with a protocol describing how this part of the geometry library is activated and used within formal proofs.

Let △ABC be a triangle.

Let E and F be the midpoints of AC and AB, respectively, and let

  G = BE ∩ CF.

The objective is to prove that G also lies on the third median of △ABC.

Finlay's key observation is that the original problem can be transformed into a statement about a parallelogram.

To achieve this, he introduces an auxiliary point P so that G is the midpoint of AP.

The remainder of the proof consists of five elementary geometric steps.

## 3. Finlay's Five-Step Argument

### Step 1 — Construction of Parallel Relations

Since F and G are the midpoints of AB and AP in △ABP,

  FG ∥ BP.

Because C, F and G are collinear,

  CG ∥ BP.

Similarly, since E and G are the midpoints of AC and AP in △ACP,

  EG ∥ CP,

and therefore

  BG ∥ CP.

This step belongs entirely to the theory of midsegments and parallel lines.

From the perspective of the formal proof, its role is to construct the parallel relations required for recognizing a parallelogram.

---

### Step 2 — Recognition of the Parallelogram

Since

  CG ∥ BP

and

  BG ∥ CP,

quadrilateral BPCG is a parallelogram.

This is the recognition phase of the proof. Once the configuration has been identified as a parallelogram, the subsequent reasoning proceeds almost entirely by using properties of the constructed parallelogram.

---

### Step 3 — Construction of the Diagonal Configuration

Let

  D = AP ∩ BC.

Since AP and BC are the diagonals of parallelogram BPCG, the geometric configuration required for the diagonal theorem is now complete.

This step prepares the application of the diagonal theorem but does not yet use any property of the parallelogram.

---

### Step 4 — Application of the Parallelogram Diagonal Theorem

The proof now applies the fundamental theorem stating that the diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other.

Hence D is the midpoint of BC.

Therefore AD is a median of △ABC.

This is the key application of the reusable parallelogram component.

---

### Step 5 — Derivation of the Third Median

Because G lies on AP and AP coincides with the median AD,

the point G belongs to the third median.

Consequently, the three medians of △ABC are concurrent.

At this stage the proof returns from the parallelogram argument to the original centroid theorem.

---

## 4. From Proof to Reusable Reasoning

Although Finlay's proof is presented as a sequence of elementary geometric arguments, the Lean formalization reveals an additional structural layer.

The proof repeatedly follows the same reasoning pattern.

| Finlay step | Role in the formal proof |
| :--- | :--- |
| Step 1 | Construct the parallel relations |
| Step 2 | Recognize the parallelogram |
| Step 3 | Construct the diagonal configuration |
| Step 4 | Apply the parallelogram diagonal theorem |
| Step 5 | Derive the third median |

The Lean proof closely follows this sequence. Rather than invoking isolated lemmas, it first constructs an appropriate configuration, then recognizes a parallelogram, applies its diagonal theorem, and finally returns to the original geometric argument.

## 5. Impact on the Geometry Library

The most important consequence of the formalization was not merely the identification of reusable parallelogram lemmas.

More significantly, the formal proof revealed a reusable pattern for employing this part of the geometry library.

The reusable parallelogram component consists of two complementary parts.

The first contains the mathematical results themselves:

- characterization of parallelograms,
- properties of opposite sides,
- diagonal theorems,
- midpoint results,
- reusable construction lemmas.

The second describes the typical sequence in which these results are used inside formal proofs:

- construction of parallel relations,
- recognition of the parallelogram,
- construction of the diagonal configuration,
- application of the diagonal theorem,
- derivation of the required geometric consequence.

This viewpoint changes the role of formalization within the project.

Rather than verifying isolated theorems, formalization becomes a method for discovering reusable mathematical components together with recurring reasoning patterns describing how they are used.

## 6. Towards Reusable Parallelogram Components

The next stage of the project is no longer concerned solely with the centroid theorem.

Instead, the objective is to further develop the reusable parallelogram component extracted from Finlay's proof.

This development has two complementary objectives.

The first is mathematical:

- characterization of parallelograms,
- properties of opposite sides,
- diagonal theorems,
- midpoint properties,
- congruence results,
- reusable synthetic constructions.

The second is methodological:

to understand and document how these mathematical results are employed inside formal proofs.

In Finlay's proof, the reusable parallelogram component is employed according to the following sequence:

1. Construction of parallel relations.
2. Recognition of the parallelogram.
3. Construction of the diagonal configuration.
4. Application of the parallelogram diagonal theorem.
5. Derivation of the third median.

The same reasoning pattern is expected to reappear in many synthetic proofs beyond Finlay's theorem.

The formal Lean development therefore suggests that reusable mathematical knowledge should be organized not only as definitions and theorems, but also together with recurring patterns describing how these results are used inside formal proofs.
