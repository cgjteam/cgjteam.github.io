# Log 008 — What Finlay Did Not Need

## Negative Knowledge Revealed by Formalization

### Introduction

Formalization is usually described as a process of adding precision to mathematics.

A mathematical idea is translated into exact definitions, assumptions and proofs.

However, the Finlay formalization revealed another important aspect.

Formalization does not only show what a proof requires.

It also shows what the proof does not require.

This negative information is often difficult to see in ordinary mathematical practice.

A classical proof is usually embedded in a much larger mathematical environment containing many familiar concepts. Formalization separates the essential logical structure from the surrounding mathematical intuition.

The purpose of this log is to document what the Finlay proof did not need.

---

## The Difference Between Intuition and Necessity

When reading a classical geometry proof, a mathematician naturally brings a large amount of background knowledge.

A triangle suggests:

- lengths,
- angles,
- similarity,
- area,
- coordinates,
- transformations.

A segment suggests:

- endpoints,
- distance,
- order,
- interior points.

These ideas belong naturally to Euclidean geometry.

However, a formal proof asks a more precise question:

Which of these concepts are actually used?

The answer can be surprisingly small.

---

## No Coordinate System

The Finlay proof does not require coordinates.

There is no need to represent points as pairs:

```text
(x,y)
```

There are no equations describing lines.

There are no calculations involving slopes.

The proof never asks where a point is located numerically.

Instead, it uses only relational statements:

- points are collinear,
- segments are parallel,
- a point is a midpoint,
- a quadrilateral is a parallelogram.

The theorem therefore lives at a purely synthetic level.

---

## No Metric Structure

The proof also does not require distances.

Although midpoint is often explained using equality of lengths, the formal development does not compute any length.

The midpoint relation is treated as a geometric primitive.

The proof uses consequences of midpoint:

- collinearity,
- construction of midsegments,
- identification of median points.

The numerical meaning of distance remains outside the dependency graph.

This observation is important.

A concept may have a rich intuitive interpretation while only a small part of that interpretation is required formally.

---

## No Angle Theory

Another surprising absence is angle theory.

The proof does not use:

- angle equality,
- angle addition,
- angle measurement,
- similarity criteria.

The Midsegment Theorem is used as an established synthetic mechanism.

Once available, it provides the necessary parallel relations without introducing angle reasoning.

This means that the formal dependency structure of Finlay is different from many textbook proofs of related results.

---

## No Similarity Theory

Many classical approaches to median theorems rely on similar triangles.

The ratio:

\[
2:1
\]

between the centroid and the vertices is often proved through similarity.

The Finlay construction follows a different route.

It creates an auxiliary point and uses a parallelogram.

The essential chain is:

```
midpoints

    |

midsegment parallelism

    |

parallelogram

    |

diagonal midpoint property

    |

median
```

Similarity theory is therefore mathematically relevant to the general subject, but not logically necessary for this proof.

---

## No Betweenness Assumptions

One of the most interesting observations concerns order.

Classical geometry often distinguishes:

- a point on a line,
- a point inside a segment,
- a point between two other points.

The current formalization contains a notion of betweenness:

```lean
Between
```

but the Finlay proof does not use it.

The proof only requires collinearity.

This means that the argument does not depend on the order structure of a line.

The formal theorem is therefore valid at a more abstract level than the original geometric picture might suggest.

---

## No Construction Geometry

The original synthetic argument uses an auxiliary point.

A classical geometer may interpret this as a construction with ruler and compass.

The formal proof, however, does not formalize the construction process itself.

It assumes the relevant point relations and studies their consequences.

This separates two different questions:

1. Can an object be constructed?
2. What follows from the existence of that object?

Formal proof assistants often focus on the second question.

---

## The Mathematical Meaning of Absence

The absence of these concepts is not a weakness.

It is information about the theorem.

The Finlay theorem belongs to a structural layer of geometry based on:

- incidence,
- parallelism,
- midpoint relations,
- parallelogram properties.

It does not belong primarily to:

- metric geometry,
- analytic geometry,
- angle geometry,
- computational geometry.

Formalization therefore acts as a tool for discovering the natural mathematical habitat of a theorem.

---

## Consequences for Library Development

This observation influences the design of the library.

Adding every classical geometric concept immediately would create a large but poorly structured environment.

A better strategy is to introduce concepts when they are justified by actual mathematical dependencies.

The formal development suggests a principle:

> A geometry library should grow from necessary structures, not from the complete vocabulary of geometry textbooks.

New concepts should appear when new proofs require them.
