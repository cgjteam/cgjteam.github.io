# Log 008 — The Dependency Graph of Finlay's Proof

### Introduction
The previous logs examined the individual components of Ian Finlay's synthetic proof. Each theorem, construction, and geometric property was analyzed separately. The objective of this log is to assemble these components into a single dependency graph. Rather than following the chronological order of the proof, we describe its logical architecture.

### The Dependency Structure
The proof depends on four levels of mathematical knowledge:

* **Level 1 — Definitions:** Triangle, Midpoint, Median, Parallel lines, Parallelogram. (These provide the vocabulary).
* **Level 2 — Elementary Theorems:** Midsegment Theorem, AA Similarity Criterion, Diagonals of a Parallelogram Bisect Each Other.
* **Level 3 — Intermediate Results:**
    * Intersection point $G$ divides two medians in the ratio $2:1$.
    * Construction of point $P$.
    * Quadrilateral $BGCP$ is a parallelogram.
    * The midpoint of $BC$ lies on line $AG$.
* **Level 4 — Final Theorem:** The third median passes through the point $G$ (Concurrency).



### Logical Flow
The dependency graph can be summarized as:
$$\text{Definitions} \rightarrow \text{Elementary Theorems} \rightarrow \text{Intermediate Results} \rightarrow \text{Concurrency Theorem}$$

This representation emphasizes that the proof is not a linear sequence of calculations but a hierarchy of logical dependencies.

### Why Build a Dependency Graph?
Classical mathematical texts present proofs as continuous narratives, but formal verification requires a different perspective: every inference must depend explicitly on previously established statements. The dependency graph provides this blueprint. It identifies the minimal logical prerequisites of each step and serves as the foundation for future formalization.
