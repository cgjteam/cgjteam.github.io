# Log 003 — Why Finlay's Proof Is Different

### Introduction
Many classical proofs of the concurrency of the medians rely on coordinates, vectors, affine geometry, or barycentric coordinates. Although these methods are efficient, they introduce algebraic machinery that is external to elementary Euclidean geometry.

Ian Finlay's construction follows a different philosophy. The proof remains entirely synthetic and is built from a surprisingly small collection of elementary geometric ideas. Understanding why this is possible is the central motivation for the analysis carried out in this project.

### A Minimal Synthetic Construction
At first sight, Finlay's proof appears almost too short. Its central construction consists of only a few geometric operations:

* Selecting two medians.
* Extending one median.
* Applying the Midsegment Theorem.
* Recognizing a parallelogram.
* Using the midpoint property of its diagonals.



The argument proceeds entirely through elementary incidence, parallelism, and midpoint relations. No coordinates, vector calculations, or affine transformations are introduced.

### Why Analyze the Proof?
A short proof is not necessarily a simple proof. Many concise arguments hide logical assumptions that remain invisible during ordinary mathematical reading. For a formal proof assistant, however, every assumption must be stated explicitly. The purpose of this project is therefore to determine the exact logical cost of Finlay's construction:

* Which facts are genuinely required?
* Which statements are consequences of earlier lemmas?
* Which steps are mathematically obvious only because they are left implicit?

### Research Perspective
The objective is not to improve Finlay's proof, but to expose its internal architecture. Each subsequent log will isolate one elementary component of the construction and examine it independently before returning to the complete argument.
