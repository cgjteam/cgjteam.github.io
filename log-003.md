# Log-003 — Encapsulating Synthetic Arguments

**How the first reusable mathematical components emerged during the formal verification of Finlay's proof.**

**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** Mathematical Development

---

## 1. Overview

The formal verification of Finlay's proof revealed that several parts of the argument deserved to become independent mathematical components.

Rather than remaining embedded inside a single proof, these constructions were gradually promoted to reusable theorems.

At the same time, the repeated use of parallelograms suggested the first organizational decisions concerning their future theory.

---

## 2. The First Encapsulated Argument

The first complete argument extracted from the proof became the theorem

```lean
MidsegmentParallel
```

### Mathematical content

If two points are the midpoints of two sides of a triangle, then the segment joining them is parallel to the third side.

### Project decision

Instead of reproducing its synthetic construction whenever needed, the complete proof was encapsulated inside a single theorem.

```lean
have hFGparallelBP :=
  MidsegmentParallel
    B P A F G
    hFB hGP
```

This was the first theorem introduced primarily for reuse rather than for a single application.

---

## 3. The First Appearance of Parallelogram Theory

The formalization also revealed that the same geometric object appeared twice in independent arguments.

### Inside `MidsegmentParallel`

A temporary parallelogram is constructed as an auxiliary object.

Its opposite sides provide the parallelism required to complete the Midsegment Theorem.

### Inside `FinlayProof`

A second parallelogram is constructed independently.

This time the crucial property is that its diagonals bisect one another, leading to the midpoint needed in the final step of the proof.

The repeated appearance of the same construction suggested that the theory of parallelograms should gradually become independent of Finlay's proof.

---

## 4. First Working Description of a Parallelogram

At this stage the project deliberately avoids developing the complete theory of parallelograms.

Instead, several equivalent descriptions are accepted as interchangeable working statements.

This decision is reflected by the first characterization theorems introduced into the library.

| Theorem | Mathematical content |
|---------|----------------------|
| `ParallelogramParallelCharacterization` | A parallelogram is described by two pairs of opposite parallel sides. |
| `ParallelogramCongruentCharacterizationPrim` | Opposite sides of a parallelogram are congruent. |
| `ParallelogramDiagonalCharacterizationPrim` | The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect one another. |
| `OnePairParallelCongruentRecognition` | One pair of opposite sides being parallel and congruent is sufficient to recognize a parallelogram. |

These theorems are treated as the first interface of the emerging theory.

Their systematic development is postponed to the next stage of the project.

---

## 5. New Components Extracted from Finlay's Proof

Besides `MidsegmentParallel`, the formalization produced several additional reusable components.

| Theorem | Mathematical role |
|---------|-------------------|
| `ParallelogramFromFinlayParallelPrim` | Recognizes a parallelogram from the two pairs of parallel sides constructed in Finlay's proof. |
| `FinlayParallelogramStep` | Uses the diagonal property of a parallelogram to conclude that D is the midpoint of BC. |

Both theorems originate in Finlay's proof but naturally belong to the developing theory of parallelograms.

They are therefore regarded as transitional results that will eventually be absorbed into that theory.

---

## 6. Naming Decisions

The introduction of reusable mathematical components also changed the naming policy.

Whenever possible, theorem names now describe their mathematical content rather than the proof in which they first appeared.

Examples include

- `MidsegmentParallel`,
- `ParallelogramParallelCharacterization`,
- `OnePairParallelCongruentRecognition`.

This convention will guide the future organization of the geometry library.

---

## 7. Outlook

The first reusable components have now been separated from Finlay's proof.

The next stage of the project will focus on transforming these components into a coherent formal theory of parallelograms.
