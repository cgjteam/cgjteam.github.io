# Log-003 — Encapsulating Synthetic Arguments

**How classical geometric reasoning gradually evolved into reusable formal theorems during the verification of Finlay's proof.**

**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** Formalization Methodology

---

## 1. Overview

The previous logs documented the mathematics of the project.

Log-001 reconstructed Finlay's synthetic proof, while Log-002 examined the two mathematical components on which it depends.

The present document describes the next stage of the project.

Rather than introducing new geometry, it records the first design decisions that emerged during the formalization process. These decisions were not planned in advance. They resulted directly from translating classical synthetic reasoning into formal mathematics.

---

## 2. From Proofs to Reusable Arguments

At first glance, formalization appears to consist of translating theorems.

The development of Finlay's proof quickly demonstrated that this view is incomplete.

Many classical arguments are repeatedly used without being recognized as independent mathematical objects. A geometer naturally treats them as a single reasoning step, while a formal proof requires each step to be represented explicitly.

The project therefore gradually shifted its emphasis.

Instead of formalizing isolated results, it began identifying recurring synthetic arguments and encapsulating them as reusable theorems.

---

## 3. Design Decision 1 — Encapsulating Complete Arguments

The Midsegment Theorem became the first example of this process.

Initially, its proof appeared as an intermediate part of Finlay's argument.

However, once completed, exactly the same argument was required twice without modification.

Instead of repeating the entire construction, the project introduced the theorem

```lean
have hFGparallelBP :=
  MidsegmentParallel ...
```

The complete synthetic argument became a single reusable theorem.

This represented the first step towards an independent geometry library.

---

## 4. Design Decision 2 — Making Implicit Geometry Explicit

Formalization also exposed reasoning steps that are usually left implicit in handwritten geometry.

For example, after obtaining

```lean
FG ∥ BP
```

the proof immediately continues with

```lean
CG ∥ BP
```

because points C, F and G are collinear.

In classical geometry this transformation is almost automatic.

During formalization it became an explicit theorem.

```lean
have hCGparallelBP :=
  collinear_parallel_substitute
    F G C B P
    ...
```

The same phenomenon appeared repeatedly throughout the development.

Small lemmas describing elementary synthetic transformations gradually formed a reusable layer connecting the principal geometric theorems.

---

## 5. Design Decision 3 — Naming by Mathematics

The first theorem names naturally reflected the history of the project.

For example, a theorem discovered while formalizing Finlay's proof initially received a name referring to that proof.

As the library expanded, this convention became increasingly misleading.

A theorem describing a property of parallelograms belongs to the theory of parallelograms, regardless of where it first appeared.

The naming policy was therefore revised.

Names were chosen to describe mathematical content rather than historical origin.

This decision significantly clarified the emerging structure of the library.

---

## 6. Design Decision 4 — Discovering the Library

An unexpected observation emerged during development.

The project never began with the intention of constructing a geometry library.

Instead, the library appeared naturally.

Each completed proof suggested reusable arguments.

Each reusable argument became an independent theorem.

Each theorem gradually strengthened the mathematical infrastructure available for subsequent proofs.

The architecture of the library was therefore discovered rather than designed.

---

## 7. Outlook

The first three logs describe a natural progression.

- Log-001 documents Finlay's proof.
- Log-002 examines the mathematics supporting that proof.
- Log-003 records the first methodological decisions that transformed those mathematical ideas into reusable formal components.

Subsequent work will continue this process by extending the supporting theory while preserving the synthetic style of the original geometry.
