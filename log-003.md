# Log-003 — Encapsulating Synthetic Arguments

**How complete synthetic arguments gradually became reusable mathematical components.**

**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** Mathematical Development

---

## 1. Overview

The previous log described the mathematical ideas underlying the formal verification of Finlay's proof.

The next stage consisted in identifying those arguments that deserved to become independent mathematical components.

Instead of reconstructing the same synthetic reasoning whenever it appeared, complete arguments were gradually encapsulated as reusable theorems.

---

## 2. The First Encapsulated Argument

The first theorem extracted from the development was

<pre class="formal">
MidsegmentParallel
</pre>

**Geometric statement**

- D is the midpoint of AC,
- E is the midpoint of BC,

therefore

- DE ∥ AB.

Rather than reconstructing the proof each time, the complete synthetic argument became available through a single theorem.

Its later use inside `FinlayProof` reduces to

<pre class="formal">
have hFGparallelBP :=
  MidsegmentParallel
    B P A F G
    hFB hGP
</pre>

The entire geometric construction has disappeared behind a single mathematical name.

---

## 3. Encapsulating the Parallelogram

While proving `MidsegmentParallel`, an auxiliary parallelogram is introduced.

Its geometric role is immediate.

- opposite sides of a parallelogram are parallel;

hence the desired parallelism follows directly from the auxiliary construction.

Later, the formalization of `FinlayProof` introduces another parallelogram.

This time a different property is required.

- the diagonals of a parallelogram bisect one another.

The repeated appearance of the same geometric object suggested that these properties should become independent reusable results rather than remain hidden inside individual proofs.

---

## 4. First Characterizations

The first reusable descriptions of a parallelogram were introduced as separate theorems.

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramParallelCharacterization
</pre>

**Geometric statement**

AB ∥ CD

BC ∥ AD

⇔

ABCD is a parallelogram.

---

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramDiagonalCharacterizationPrim
</pre>

**Geometric statement**

The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect one another.

---

<pre class="formal">
OnePairParallelCongruentRecognition
</pre>

**Geometric statement**

AB ∥ CD

AB ≅ CD

⇒

ABCD is a parallelogram.

These theorems formed the first working interface of the emerging parallelogram theory.

At this stage their purpose was practical: to support further formalization without repeatedly reconstructing elementary arguments.

---

## 5. Naming Decisions

Encapsulation also changed the naming policy.

Theorem names began to describe mathematical objects rather than the proofs in which they first appeared.

For example,

<pre class="formal">
MidsegmentParallel
</pre>

refers to the Midsegment Theorem itself,

while

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramParallelCharacterization
</pre>

describes one particular characterization of parallelograms.

The names therefore reflect mathematical meaning rather than historical origin.

---

## 6. Outlook

The first stage of encapsulation transformed several complete synthetic arguments into reusable mathematical components.

The next stage will organize these isolated components into a coherent theory of parallelograms.
