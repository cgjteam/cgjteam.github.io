# Log-003 — Encapsulating Synthetic Arguments

**How classical geometric reasoning gradually evolved into reusable formal theorems during the verification of Finlay's proof.**

**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** Formalization Methodology

---

## 1. Overview

The first two logs documented the mathematical development of the project.

Log-001 reconstructed Finlay's synthetic proof, while Log-002 examined the mathematical ideas on which it relies.

This document records the first architectural decisions that emerged during formalization.

None of these decisions were planned in advance. They appeared naturally as classical synthetic reasoning was translated into a formal mathematical language.

---

## 2. From Proofs to Mathematical Components

At first glance, formalization appears to consist simply of translating existing theorems.

The development of Finlay's proof quickly demonstrated that this view is incomplete.

Many classical arguments represent complete reasoning patterns rather than isolated mathematical facts. In handwritten geometry these patterns remain almost invisible, while formal verification requires them to become explicit.

The project therefore gradually shifted its emphasis.

Instead of translating individual theorems, it began identifying reusable synthetic arguments and turning them into independent mathematical components.

---

## 3. Design Decision 1 — Encapsulating Complete Arguments

The Midsegment Theorem became the first example of this process.

Initially, its proof appeared only as one intermediate step in the verification of Finlay's argument.

After completing the theorem, however, exactly the same construction was required twice without modification.

Instead of reproducing the entire proof, the complete synthetic argument became a reusable theorem.

<pre class="formal">
have hFGparallelBP :=
  MidsegmentParallel
    B P A F G
    hFB hGP
</pre>

The theorem no longer represented a single result.

It represented an entire geometric argument.

This marked the first step towards an independent synthetic geometry library.

---

## 4. Design Decision 2 — Making Implicit Geometry Explicit

Formalization also exposed reasoning steps that are almost invisible in classical proofs.

For example, after proving

<pre class="formal">
FG ∥ BP
</pre>

the proof immediately continues with

<pre class="formal">
CG ∥ BP
</pre>

because points C, F and G are collinear.

A geometer performs this transformation almost automatically.

Formal verification required the transformation itself to become an independent theorem.

<pre class="formal">
have hCGparallelBP :=
  collinear_parallel_substitute
    F G C B P
    ...
</pre>

Many similar lemmas gradually appeared.

Although individually elementary, together they formed the connective layer joining the principal geometric theorems.

---

## 5. Design Decision 3 — Naming by Mathematics

Another important decision concerned theorem names.

The earliest names naturally reflected the history of development.

<pre class="formal">
-- Early version

FinlayParallelogramStep

-- Current version

ParallelogramDiagonalMidpoint
</pre>

The first name described where the theorem had been discovered.

The second described where it belongs mathematically.

This principle was gradually adopted throughout the library.

As a consequence, the structure of the code increasingly reflected the structure of geometry rather than the chronology of the project.

---

## 6. Design Decision 4 — Discovering the Library

Perhaps the most unexpected observation was that the library itself was never designed.

It emerged naturally.

Each completed proof suggested reusable arguments.

Each reusable argument became an independent theorem.

Each theorem strengthened the mathematical infrastructure available for subsequent developments.

The architecture of the library therefore evolved directly from the mathematics.

---

## 7. Outlook

The first three logs describe three consecutive stages of the project.

- **Log-001** documents Finlay's proof.
- **Log-002** examines the mathematics supporting that proof.
- **Log-003** records the formalization decisions that transformed recurring synthetic arguments into reusable mathematical components.

Future work will continue extending this emerging theory while preserving the synthetic character of the original geometry.
