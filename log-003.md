# Log-003 — Encapsulating Synthetic Arguments

**How synthetic arguments gradually became reusable mathematical components.**

**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** Mathematical Development

---

## 1. Overview

After completing the mathematical work described in the previous logs, the focus shifted from proving new statements to organizing the existing ones.

Several arguments already established during the formalization appeared suitable for independent use.

Rather than leaving them inside larger proofs, we began extracting them as named mathematical components.

This log records the first examples of this encapsulation process.

---

## 2. Encapsulating the Midsegment Argument

The first theorem extracted from the development was

<pre class="formal">
MidsegmentParallel
</pre>

Its purpose was to isolate the complete proof of the Midsegment Theorem before continuing with the formalization of `FinlayProof`.

Once introduced, the theorem replaced the entire construction by a single invocation.

<pre class="formal">
have hFGparallelBP :=
  MidsegmentParallel
    B P A F G
    hFB hGP
</pre>

From this point onward, the proof no longer refers to the auxiliary construction.

Only the mathematical conclusion remains visible.

This was the first complete synthetic argument encapsulated in the library.

---

## 3. Encapsulating the Auxiliary Parallelogram

While proving `MidsegmentParallel`, an auxiliary parallelogram is constructed.

Its purpose is not to study parallelograms themselves, but to transform the required statement into an immediate consequence of a familiar property:

> opposite sides of a parallelogram are parallel.

Later, during the formalization of `FinlayProof`, a parallelogram appears once again.

This time, however, a different property is required:

> the diagonals of a parallelogram bisect one another.

Thus the same geometric object appears naturally in two independent arguments, each time contributing a different property.

This observation motivated the decision to stop treating these properties as isolated proof steps.

Instead, they gradually became independent mathematical components.

---

## 4. First Working Characterizations

The repeated use of parallelograms also required an organizational decision.

Rather than interrupting the development to prove the equivalence of every classical characterization, the project temporarily adopted several standard descriptions as interchangeable working statements.

The first theorems reflecting this decision include

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramParallelCharacterization
</pre>

which exposes the parallel-side characterization,

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramDiagonalCharacterizationPrim
</pre>

which provides the diagonal property,

and

<pre class="formal">
OnePairParallelCongruentRecognition
</pre>

which recognizes a parallelogram from one pair of opposite sides that are both parallel and congruent.

At this stage these theorems form a practical working interface rather than a complete formal theory.

---

## 5. Naming the New Components

Once an argument became reusable, it also received an independent mathematical name.

For example,

<pre class="formal">
MidsegmentParallel
</pre>

no longer refers to its first appearance inside `FinlayProof`.

Likewise,

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramParallelCharacterization
</pre>

describes a mathematical characterization rather than a particular proof step.

The naming policy therefore follows mathematical meaning instead of historical origin.

---

## 6. Outlook

The formalization has now reached the point where complete synthetic arguments begin to disappear behind reusable theorem names.

The next stage will develop these isolated components into a coherent theory of parallelograms, replacing the temporary working interface by systematically proved equivalences.
