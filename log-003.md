# Extracting Synthetic Proof Components

**How a classical synthetic proof was decomposed into reusable formal arguments.**

**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** Mathematical Development

---

## 1. Overview

The previous logs focused on the mathematical foundations of Finlay's synthetic proof and its formal verification.

After completing the first formal proof, a new structural question appeared.

Which parts of the argument represent reusable mathematical ideas, and which parts belong only to this particular proof?

The first answer was not the construction of a complete geometric theory.

Instead, the proof itself was decomposed into independent synthetic components.

---

## 2. From Proof Script to Mathematical Components

The original Finlay argument is short when written informally.

However, formalization reveals several logically independent steps:

1. Construction of parallel relations using the Midsegment Theorem.
2. Recognition of a parallelogram.
3. Transfer of an intersection point to a diagonal.
4. Extraction of a midpoint from diagonal bisection.
5. Identification of the third median.

Each of these steps contains a separate mathematical idea.

The formal development therefore introduced the following reusable theorems:

```lean

FinlayStep2
FinlayStep3
FinlayStep4
FinlayStep5
```

## 3. The Midsegment Argument

The first reusable component formalizes the application of the Midsegment Theorem inside Finlay's construction.

The theorem

```lean
FinlayStep1
```

combines two independent applications of

```lean
MidsegmentTheorem
```

to obtain:

\[
CG \parallel BP
\]

and

\[
BG \parallel CP.
\]

The original geometric reasoning remains visible, while the formal proof no longer requires reconstructing the intermediate steps.

## 4. The Parallelogram Construction

The second component isolates the recognition of the parallelogram.

The theorem


two pairs of opposite parallel sides

This step identifies the central geometric object of Finlay's construction.

## 5. Intersection and Diagonal Structure

\[

\[

The theorem

```lean
FinlayStep4
```

the diagonals bisect each other.

## 6. The Final Median Step

The last component isolates the elementary definition of a median.

The theorem

```lean
FinlayStep5
```

states that if

\[
D
\]

is the midpoint of

\[
BC,
\]

then

\[
AD
\]

is a median.

The final theorem

```lean
Finlay
```

combines all five components into the complete concurrency proof.

It establishes:

- `AD` is a median of triangle `ABC`,
- `A`, `G`, and `D` are collinear.

Therefore the point `G` lies on the third median, completing the proof that the three medians of a triangle are concurrent.

---

## 7. Toward Reusable Geometric Theories

The current stage does not yet constitute a complete theory of parallelograms or triangle geometry.

Instead, it demonstrates a methodology:

1. Formalize a classical synthetic proof.
2. Identify independent mathematical steps.
3. Extract reusable components.
4. Gradually organize them into broader theories.

The formalization of Finlay's proof provides the first example of this process.

The next stage of the project will investigate which extracted components deserve to become independent geometric theories.
