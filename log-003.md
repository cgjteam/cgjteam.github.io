# Log-003 — Encapsulating Synthetic Arguments

**How complete synthetic arguments gradually became reusable mathematical components.**

**Date:** 2026-07-13  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** Mathematical Development

---

## 1. Overview

The previous logs focused on the mathematics underlying the formal verification of Finlay's proof.

Once these arguments had been established, a new question naturally emerged.

Which parts of the development should remain local proof steps, and which deserved to become independent mathematical results?

The present log documents the first stage of this transition.

Instead of repeatedly reconstructing the same synthetic arguments, they gradually became reusable components of the developing geometry library.

---

## 2. The First Encapsulated Argument

The first theorem extracted from the development was

<pre class="formal">
MidsegmentParallel A C B D E
</pre>

The theorem concerns a triangle whose vertices are `A`, `B` and `C`, together with the midpoints `D` of `AC` and `E` of `BC`.

Geometrically, it states that

- if `D` is the midpoint of `AC`,
- and `E` is the midpoint of `BC`,

then

- `DE ∥ AB`.

The theorem encapsulates the complete synthetic proof of this classical result.

Once introduced, the proof itself disappears from subsequent developments.

For example, inside `FinlayProof` the entire construction is replaced by the single invocation

<pre class="formal">
have hFGparallelBP :=
  MidsegmentParallel
    B P A F G
    hFB hGP
</pre>

The surrounding proof no longer refers to the auxiliary construction.

Only the mathematical conclusion — the parallelism `FG ∥ BP` — remains visible.

This was the first complete synthetic argument encapsulated as an independent theorem.

---

## 3. Encapsulating the Auxiliary Parallelogram

The proof of `MidsegmentParallel` contains a second important idea.

Instead of proving the required parallelism directly, an auxiliary parallelogram is constructed.

The construction immediately reduces the problem to the elementary property

> opposite sides of a parallelogram are parallel.

The same geometric object appears once again during the formal verification of `FinlayProof`.

This time, however, a different property is required.

Rather than using the opposite sides, the proof relies on the fact that

> the diagonals of a parallelogram bisect one another.

Thus two independent arguments employ the same geometric object while using different parts of its theory.

This repeated appearance suggested that the properties of parallelograms should gradually become independent reusable theorems instead of remaining embedded inside individual proofs.

---

## 4. First Working Characterizations

The repeated appearance of parallelograms in independent arguments naturally led to the first reusable descriptions of this geometric object.

Rather than repeatedly proving the same elementary properties, they were promoted to independent theorems.

The first characterization introduced into the library was

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramParallelCharacterization A B C D
</pre>

The theorem concerns the quadrilateral `ABCD`.

Geometrically, it identifies a parallelogram through its defining parallelism:

- `AB ∥ CD`,
- `BC ∥ AD`.

This theorem became the natural entry point to the developing theory of parallelograms.

---

A second independent characterization was introduced as

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramDiagonalCharacterizationPrim A B C D
</pre>

Again, the theorem concerns the quadrilateral `ABCD`.

Its geometric content is the classical property

- the diagonals `AC` and `BD` bisect one another.

This is precisely the property required in the final stage of `FinlayProof`, but after encapsulation it becomes available independently of that proof.

---

Finally, the library introduced the recognition theorem

<pre class="formal">
OnePairParallelCongruentRecognition A B C D
</pre>

Its interface again refers to the quadrilateral `ABCD`.

Geometrically, it states that

- `AB ∥ CD`,
- `AB ≅ CD`

are sufficient to conclude that

- `ABCD` is a parallelogram.

Instead of viewing this result as a local step inside a particular proof, it becomes part of the reusable interface describing parallelograms.

At this stage these theorems should not yet be regarded as a complete formal theory.

Rather, they constitute the first working collection of reusable characterizations extracted from the ongoing formalization.

---

## 5. Naming Decisions

Encapsulation naturally led to a revision of the naming policy.

Theorem names gradually ceased to describe the historical context in which they were discovered.

Instead, they began to identify the mathematical object represented by the theorem.

For example,

<pre class="formal">
MidsegmentParallel A C B D E
</pre>

is named after the geometric theorem it represents rather than the proof in which it first appeared.

Similarly,

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramParallelCharacterization A B C D
</pre>

and

<pre class="formal">
ParallelogramDiagonalCharacterizationPrim A B C D
</pre>

are named according to the mathematical properties they describe.

This change marks the beginning of organizing the library around mathematical theories rather than around individual formal developments.

---

## 6. Outlook

The present stage completes the first process of encapsulation.

The Midsegment Theorem has become an independent reusable result.

Several fundamental properties of parallelograms have been separated from the proofs in which they originally appeared and promoted to reusable mathematical components.

The next stage of the project will no longer focus on extracting arguments from individual proofs.

Instead, it will develop these isolated components into a coherent theory of parallelograms, replacing the current working collection of characterizations by a systematic formal development.
