# LOG-002 — Local Logical Extraction

**Status:** Conceptual note  
**Date:** 2026-07-12

## 1. Motivation
During the formalization of Finlay's proof, an unexpected observation emerged: a classical geometric diagram carries far more information than is actually required by the proof. A mathematician reading a traditional proof unconsciously relies on many properties suggested by the drawing. 

Formalization forces us to distinguish between:
- Properties that are visually present in the figure.
- Properties that are logically necessary for the proof.

This distinction is one of the most valuable insights gained from this project.

## 2. Case Studies

### Midpoint
The definition of a midpoint used in the library contains only two conditions:
1. The three points are collinear.
2. The two adjacent segments are congruent.

Classically, this appears incomplete as it lacks the notion of *betweenness*. However, the formal analysis shows that no step of the proof requires the stronger statement that the midpoint lies *between* the endpoints. The richer geometric intuition is unnecessary for this theorem.

### Centroid
Intuitively, the word *centroid* evokes a collection of properties: it lies inside the triangle, it is the intersection of medians, and it divides them in a 2:1 ratio. Nevertheless, the formal proof uses only a small subset of these facts. Most of the intuitive picture never participates in the logical derivation.

## 3. Methodological Observation
This project differs from the classical axiomatization programs (Hilbert, Tarski, Szmielew). Those programs ask: 
> *What is the minimal set of axioms required to reconstruct the whole of Euclidean geometry?*

The present project asks: 
> *What is the minimal logical content required to formalize one particular proof?*

The reduction is **local**, not **global**. We minimize the assumptions actually used by a single theorem. Only when a later theorem genuinely requires a richer language do new notions become part of the formal system.

## 4. Conclusion
Formalization is more than just translating mathematics into Lean. It is an analytical instrument that reveals which assumptions truly drive a proof and which belong only to our geometric intuition. In this sense, Lean becomes a tool for discovering the logical structure hidden beneath classical geometric reasoning.
