# LOG-002 — Local Logical Extraction

**Status:** Conceptual note  
**Date:** 2026-07-12

---

## 1. Motivation
During the formalization of Finlay's proof, an unexpected observation emerged: a classical geometric diagram carries far more information than is actually required by the proof. A mathematician reading a traditional proof unconsciously relies on many properties suggested by the drawing. 

Formalization forces us to distinguish between:
- Properties that are visually present in the figure.
- Properties that are logically necessary for the proof.

This distinction is one of the most valuable insights gained from this project. Formalization acts as an analytical tool that separates human geometric intuition from the assumptions that are logically indispensable for a particular proof.

## 2. Case Studies

### Midpoint
The definition of a midpoint used in the library contains only two conditions:
1. The three points are collinear.
2. The two adjacent segments are congruent.

While classical geometry often requires a notion of *betweenness*, formal analysis confirms this is intentionally absent here. The proof never requires this stronger notion, demonstrating that the midpoint functions correctly as a locally sufficient object for this theorem without a global betweenness axiom.

### Centroid
The intuition surrounding the centroid is rich, suggesting properties like interiority, median intersection, or the 2:1 ratio. However, the formal proof relies only on a minimal subset of these facts. The centroid is treated here as a locally sufficient object, stripped of the "axiomatic baggage" that usually accompanies it in classical geometry. Most of our geometric intuition never participates in the logical derivation.

## 3. Methodological Observation
This project contrasts with classical foundational programs (e.g., Hilbert, Tarski, Szmielew), which sought the minimal set of axioms to reconstruct the *entirety* of Euclidean geometry.

Our methodology is governed by the principle: **"Logical reduction is local, not global."**

Rather than minimizing the foundations of geometry as a whole, we minimize the assumptions actually used by a single theorem. Each theorem defines its own "fragment" of geometry. New abstractions or notions are introduced into the formal system only when genuinely required by a later proof, ensuring the library remains as lean as the mathematics itself.

## 4. References: Foundations of Geometry
Our work contextualizes itself against the "global" foundations established by:

- **Hilbert, D. (1899).** *Foundations of Geometry.*
- **Tarski, A., & Beth, E. W. (1959).** *Axiomatic Foundations of Geometry.*
- **Borsuk, K., & Szmielew, W. (1960).** *Foundations of Geometry.*

## 5. Conclusion
Formalization serves as an analytical instrument that exposes the minimal logical structure hidden beneath classical reasoning. The purpose of the Geometry Library is not to reconstruct Euclidean geometry from minimal axioms, but to reveal the minimal logical structure required by individual geometric proofs.
