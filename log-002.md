# Log 002 — The Logical Structure of Finlay's Construction

## Introduction

The proof presented by Ian Finlay is remarkably concise. Its apparent simplicity, however, conceals several logical dependencies that are usually left implicit in classical geometry.

Rather than treating the proof as a single argument, we begin by decomposing it into elementary components. The objective is to determine which statements are fundamental, which are derived, and which assumptions are used without being explicitly stated. This decomposition is the first step toward a complete logical audit of the proof.

## The Structure of the Proof

At a high level, Finlay's construction consists of four stages:

1. Two medians are selected, and their intersection point \(G\) is considered.

2. A new point \(P\) is introduced by extending one median so that \(|AG| = |GP|\).

3. Parallelism obtained from the Midsegment Theorem produces a parallelogram whose diagonals identify the midpoint of the third side.

4. This midpoint is shown to lie on the line through \(A\) and \(G\), proving that the third median also passes through \(G\).

## Hidden Dependencies

A first inspection reveals that the proof relies on assumptions that are not explicitly discussed. Among them are:

* The existence of the intersection point of two medians.
* Properties of midpoints.
* The Midsegment Theorem.
* Properties of parallelograms.
* Elementary incidence and parallelism relations.

Some of these are immediate in classical geometry but must become explicit in any formal verification.

## Objective of the Analysis

* The goal of this project is not to simplify Finlay's proof.
* Instead, we seek to identify its minimal logical structure.
* Each subsequent log will isolate one component of the argument, verify its dependencies, and determine whether it can be formalized independently.
