## 1. Overview

Log-001 presented the complete synthetic proof of Ian Finlay together with its formal verification.

The purpose of the present document is to examine the mathematical foundations on which that proof relies.

Although Finlay's argument is remarkably concise, it depends on two fundamental components.

The first is the Midsegment Theorem, which provides the mechanism for constructing the necessary parallelisms. The second is the theory of parallelograms, whose properties transform those parallelisms into the desired geometric conclusion.

Together these two ideas constitute the mathematical foundation of Finlay's proof.

---

## 2. The Midsegment Theorem

The Midsegment Theorem is the principal geometric tool used throughout Finlay's argument.

Numerous synthetic proofs of this theorem are known. The project deliberately adopts a proof based on an auxiliary construction leading naturally to a parallelogram.

This choice proved particularly valuable because the same geometric mechanism reappears later in Finlay's proof.

## 3. The Chosen Proof

The proof proceeds through elementary synthetic steps.

---

### Step 1 - Auxiliary Construction

An auxiliary point is introduced to extend the construction and create a configuration suitable for applying congruence arguments.

The additional segment allows the construction of a quadrilateral whose properties can be recognized as those of a parallelogram.

---

### Step 2 - Congruence

The constructed triangles are compared using the SAS congruence criterion.

The congruence provides the necessary equalities of sides and angles required for the next geometric step.

---

### Step 3 - Consequences of Congruence

The congruence relations imply that one pair of opposite sides is both:

- parallel,
- congruent.

This is the essential geometric observation that connects the construction with parallelogram theory.

---

### Step 4 - Recognition of the Parallelogram

A quadrilateral with one pair of opposite sides simultaneously parallel and congruent is recognized as a parallelogram.

This step introduces the main geometric structure used later in Finlay's proof.

---

### Step 5 - Conclusion

The parallelogram properties imply the required parallelism relation.

This completes the proof of the Midsegment Theorem.

---

## 4. Two Applications in Finlay's Proof

Having established the Midsegment Theorem, its role in Finlay's proof becomes transparent.

The theorem is applied twice.

The first application, in triangle ABC, yields:

\[
FG \parallel BP.
\]

Using the collinearity of C, F and G, this becomes:

\[
CG \parallel BP.
\]

The second application yields:

\[
EG \parallel CP.
\]

Using the collinearity of B, E and G, this becomes:

\[
BG \parallel CP.
\]

These two independent applications produce the two pairs of opposite parallel sides required to recognize quadrilateral BPCG as a parallelogram.

## 5. Parallelogram Framework

The proof of the Midsegment Theorem already illustrates the central role played by parallelograms in synthetic geometry.

In Finlay's argument, the parallelogram appears as the structure that connects two independent parallel relations into a single geometric object.

At the current stage of the formalization, parallelogram properties are represented by a small collection of primitive geometric principles:

- recognition from parallel sides,
- symmetry of parallel relations,
- the midpoint property of diagonals.

These principles provide exactly the infrastructure required by Finlay's proof.

A complete theory of parallelograms, including systematic derivation of equivalent characterizations, will be developed in later stages of the project.
