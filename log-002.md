# LOG-002 — Local Logical Extraction of Assumptions

**Status:** Conceptual note  
**Date:** 2026-07-12

---

## 1. Motivation

During the formalization of Finlay's proof, an unexpected observation emerged: a classical geometric diagram carries far more information than is actually required by the proof.

A classical geometric diagram suggests many properties that are never explicitly stated but are nevertheless assumed by human intuition.

Formalization forces us to distinguish between:

- Properties that are visually present in the figure.
- Properties that are logically necessary for the proof.

This distinction is one of the most valuable insights gained from this project. Formalization acts as an analytical tool that separates human geometric intuition from the assumptions that are logically indispensable for a particular proof.

---

## 2. Case Studies

### Midpoint

The definition of a midpoint used in the library contains only two conditions:

1. The three points are collinear.
2. The two adjacent segments are congruent.

While classical geometry often requires a notion of *betweenness*, formal analysis confirms that this notion is intentionally absent here. The proof never requires the stronger statement that the midpoint lies between the endpoints.

The formal proof therefore reveals that the notion of betweenness belongs to our geometric intuition rather than to the logical core of this particular argument.

### Centroid

In classical geometry, the centroid is perceived as a highly structured object rather than merely the intersection of two medians. It naturally evokes many properties: interiority, concurrence of the medians, the 2:1 ratio, and the center of mass interpretation.

The formal proof, however, relies on only a small subset of these properties. Most of the geometric information suggested by the diagram never participates in the logical derivation.

## 3. Methodological Observation

This project contrasts with the classical foundational programs (e.g. Hilbert, Tarski, and Borsuk–Szmielew), whose objective was to identify the minimal axiomatic basis for the whole of Euclidean geometry.

**Principle.**

> **Logical reduction is local, not global.**

Rather than minimizing the foundations of geometry as a whole, this project minimizes the assumptions actually used by a single theorem.

Each theorem defines its own local fragment of geometry. New concepts are introduced only when they become logically necessary for subsequent proofs. The formal language therefore evolves together with the mathematics instead of being fixed in advance.

## 5. Conclusion

Formalization is more than a translation of mathematics into Lean.

It is an analytical instrument that exposes the minimal logical structure hidden beneath classical geometric reasoning.

The purpose of the Geometry Library is therefore **not** to reconstruct Euclidean geometry from a globally minimal set of axioms, but to reveal the minimal logical structure required by individual geometric proofs.
