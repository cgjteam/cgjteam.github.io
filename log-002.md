## 1. Overview

Log-001 presented the complete synthetic proof of Ian Finlay together with its formal verification.

The purpose of the present document is to examine the mathematical foundations on which that proof relies.

Although Finlay's argument is remarkably concise, it depends on two fundamental components.

The first is the Midsegment Theorem, which provides the mechanism for constructing the necessary parallelisms. The second is the theory of parallelograms, whose properties transform those parallelisms into the desired geometric conclusion.

Together these two ideas constitute the mathematical foundation of Finlay's proof.

## 2. The Midsegment Theorem

The Midsegment Theorem is the principal geometric tool used throughout Finlay's argument.

Numerous synthetic proofs of this theorem are known. The project deliberately adopts a proof based on an auxiliary construction leading naturally to a parallelogram.

This choice proved particularly valuable because the same geometric mechanism reappears later in Finlay's proof.

## 3. The Chosen Proof

The proof proceeds through five elementary synthetic steps.

---

### Step 1 — Auxiliary Construction

The segment DE is extended beyond E to a point F satisfying

  DE = EF.

The auxiliary segment BF is then constructed.

This simple construction creates the geometric configuration on which the remainder of the proof depends.

---

### Step 2 — Congruence

Consider triangles △DEC and △FEB.

Since

- CE = EB,
- DE = EF,
- ∠DEC = ∠FEB,

the triangles are congruent by the SAS criterion.

Consequently,

  △DEC ≅ △FEB.

---

### Step 3 — Consequences of Congruence

Congruence immediately yields

- BF = DC,
- ∠CDE = ∠EFB.

Because D is the midpoint of AC,

  DC = AD,

hence

  BF = AD.

Furthermore,

  BF ∥ AD.

*This is the first essential geometric consequence of the construction.*

---

### Step 4 — Recognition of the Parallelogram

Quadrilateral ABFD possesses one pair of opposite sides that are simultaneously

- parallel,
- congruent.

Therefore,

  ABFD is a parallelogram.

*This is the first appearance of parallelogram theory in the project.*

---

### Step 5 — Conclusion

Since opposite sides of a parallelogram are parallel,

  DF ∥ AB.

Because D, E and F are collinear,

  DE ∥ AB.

This completes the proof of the Midsegment Theorem.

---

## 4. Two Applications in Finlay's Proof

Having established the Midsegment Theorem, its role in Finlay's proof becomes transparent.

The theorem is applied twice.

The first application, in triangle △ABP, yields

  FG ∥ BP,

which immediately becomes

  CG ∥ BP,

using the collinearity of C, F and G.

The second application, in triangle △ACP, yields

  EG ∥ CP,

which similarly becomes

  BG ∥ CP,

using the collinearity of B, E and G.

These two independent applications produce the two pairs of opposite parallel sides required to recognize quadrilateral BPCG as a parallelogram.

## 5. A Working Theory of Parallelograms

The proof of the Midsegment Theorem already illustrates the central role played by parallelograms in the project.

For this reason, the development adopts a working framework in which a parallelogram is not tied to a single distinguished characterization. Instead, several classical properties are temporarily treated as equivalent descriptions of the same geometric object.

At the present stage, these equivalences are accepted as part of the mathematical framework supporting the formalization. Their mutual derivation will be established later as the theory develops.

This approach allows the formalization to follow the original mathematical arguments while keeping the supporting theory sufficiently flexible for future refinement.

---

## 6. Outlook

The Midsegment Theorem and the working theory of parallelograms together provide the complete mathematical infrastructure required for Finlay's proof.

The following stage of the project concentrates on transforming these mathematical ideas into reusable formal components, progressively replacing temporary assumptions by formally verified equivalences while preserving the original synthetic structure of the proof.
