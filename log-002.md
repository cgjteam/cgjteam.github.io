# Log-002 — The First Encounter with Lean

**Date:** 2026-07-12  
**Project:** CGJteam Lab  
**Status:** First Formal Geometry Theorem

---

## 1. Overview

After understanding the mathematical structure of Finlay's proof, the next objective was to express it in Lean.

At first this appeared to be a direct translation of a classical geometric argument. It soon became apparent that formalization requires much more than rewriting mathematical prose. Every implicit construction must become explicit, every auxiliary fact must be proved, and every dependency must be stated precisely.

The first encounter with Lean therefore became an exploration of the hidden structure of Finlay's proof.

---

## 2. The Midsegment Theorem

The first major obstacle appeared almost immediately.

Finlay's proof applies the Midsegment Theorem twice, treating it as a standard fact of Euclidean geometry.

For Lean, however, no such theorem existed.

Consequently, before the original proof could even begin, it became necessary to formalize the Midsegment Theorem itself.

This unexpectedly transformed an auxiliary result into the first substantial theorem of the project.

Its proof required considerably more infrastructure than originally anticipated.

Among the necessary ingredients were

- segment extension,
- construction of auxiliary triangles,
- vertical angles,
- the SAS congruence criterion,
- propagation of parallelism.

Although introduced solely for Finlay's proof, these results immediately became reusable components of the emerging geometry library.

---

## 3. The Central Observation

Once the Midsegment Theorem became available, the structure of Finlay's proof suddenly became much clearer.

The theorem is applied twice.

First, in △ABP,

  FG ∥ BP,

which immediately implies

  CG ∥ BP,

because C, F and G are collinear.

Applying exactly the same argument to △ACP yields

  EG ∥ CP,

and therefore

  BG ∥ CP.

These two independent applications of the same theorem produce the essential geometric observation:

quadrilateral BPCG is a parallelogram.

Rather than attacking the problem of concurrent medians directly, Finlay transforms it into a problem concerning the properties of a parallelogram.

This observation became the turning point of the entire formalization.

---

## 4. Lessons Learned

The first interaction with Lean fundamentally changed the development strategy.

Instead of viewing formal verification as a direct translation of mathematical text, the project gradually evolved toward identifying reusable mathematical components.

The Midsegment Theorem became the first independently verified theorem of the project.

More importantly, it demonstrated that the formalization process itself reveals mathematical structure that often remains implicit in traditional proofs.

---

## 5. Outlook

The construction of the parallelogram naturally leads to the final stage of Finlay's proof.

The next milestone is the formal verification that the diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other, completing the argument that the three medians of a triangle are concurrent.
