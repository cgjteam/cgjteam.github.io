# Log 009 — The Dependency Graph of a Synthetic Proof

## Understanding Formal Proofs as Mathematical Structures

### Introduction

The previous logs examined the geometry that emerged from the Finlay formalization.

Log-007 focused on the primitive concepts of the geometry core.

Log-008 examined the concepts that were not required by the proof.

The present log considers another consequence of formalization:

a formal proof is not only a sequence of arguments.

It is also a structured dependency network.

In ordinary mathematical writing, the order of presentation often hides the real logical architecture of a theorem.

Formalization makes this architecture explicit.

---

## A Classical Proof as a Dependency Structure

The informal statement of Finlay's theorem is simple:

> The three medians of a triangle are concurrent.

However, the formal proof reveals a much richer internal structure.

The final theorem depends on several intermediate layers:

```
Primitive geometric relations

        |

Elementary transformations

        |

Synthetic mechanisms

        |

Parallelogram properties

        |

Finlay theorem
```

The proof is therefore not a linear chain only.

It is a mathematical dependency graph.

---

---

## Step 1: Midpoints Generate Parallelism

The first essential transition is:

```
midpoints
     |
     v
Midsegment Theorem
     |
     v
parallelism
```

The midpoint assumptions do not immediately produce the median.

Instead, they create a new geometric structure:

parallel segments.

This is the first major transformation of the proof.

The midpoint information is converted into affine information.

---

## Step 2: Parallelism Generates a Parallelogram

The next transition is:

```
parallel relations
        |
        v
parallelogram recognition
```

The two parallel relations obtained from the Midsegment Theorem are combined to identify the auxiliary quadrilateral as a parallelogram.

This step changes the nature of the argument.

The proof is no longer about a triangle alone.

It moves into the theory of parallelograms.

---

## Step 3: The Parallelogram Produces the Midpoint

The central property used later is:

```
parallelogram

      |

diagonals bisect each other

      |

midpoint of BC
```

This is the hidden engine of Finlay's construction.

The auxiliary point P was introduced precisely to create a parallelogram whose diagonal structure forces the midpoint of the third side.

---

## Step 4: The Midpoint Produces the Median

The final transition is:

```
midpoint of BC

       |

median definition

       |

third median
```

Once D is identified as the midpoint of BC, the remaining conclusion becomes immediate.

The original problem about three medians has been transformed into a problem about the internal structure of a parallelogram.

---

## Proof Length and Mathematical Depth

One interesting observation is that the final Lean theorem is relatively short compared with the amount of mathematical structure hidden underneath.

This is not accidental.

Reusable components compress proofs.

A theorem such as:

```lean
MidsegmentTheorem
```

contains an entire synthetic argument.

A theorem such as:

```lean
ParallelogramDiagonals
```

contains another classical result.

The final proof becomes shorter because the library has absorbed mathematical knowledge.

---

## Dependency Graphs as Library Design Tools

The dependency graph is not only a way of understanding existing proofs.

It also provides guidance for future library development.

If many independent theorems depend on the same concept, that concept probably deserves its own theory.

For example:

```
Midpoint

   |

Midsegment

   |

Parallel constructions
```

suggests that midpoint theory should become a central module.

Similarly:

```
Parallelogram

   |

Diagonal properties

   |

Affine constructions
```

suggests the development of an independent parallelogram theory.

---

## Beyond Individual Proofs

Formal mathematics introduces a new way of reading mathematical texts.

A classical proof asks:

> Why is this statement true?

A formal development adds another question:

> What is the exact structure that makes this statement true?

The dependency graph answers the second question.

It reveals the architecture behind the argument.
