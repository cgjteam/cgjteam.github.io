# Log 009 — Towards a Minimal Dependency Set

### Introduction
The dependency graph constructed in the previous log identifies all explicit components of Ian Finlay's proof. A natural question follows: are all of these components indispensable? Or can some of them be replaced, simplified, or derived from more elementary principles? This log distinguishes between necessary dependencies and implementation choices.

### Necessary Dependencies vs. Derived Components
Some components are fundamental; without them, the proof cannot proceed. These include the definition of a midpoint, the Midsegment Theorem, and the properties of parallelograms.

Conversely, other elements are not independent mathematical facts, but rather implementation steps:
* The introduction of point $P$.
* The construction of quadrilateral $BGCP$.
* The identification of the midpoint of $BC$.

These are creative choices in Finlay's argument, not external prerequisites.

### Hidden Assumptions
Our analysis also reveals assumptions that are rarely stated explicitly, such as:
* The existence and uniqueness of the intersection point of two medians.
* The non-degeneracy of the triangle.

Such assumptions do not constitute the main argument, yet they are essential for its logical completeness.

### A First Approximation of the Minimal Core
The analysis suggests that Finlay's proof depends on a surprisingly small mathematical core:
1. Midpoint constructions.
2. One theorem relating midpoints to parallelism.
3. One theorem concerning parallelograms.
4. Elementary incidence properties.

Everything else is produced by the construction itself. 

### Why This Matters
Determining the minimal dependency set serves two purposes. First, it clarifies the internal architecture of the proof. Second, it provides a precise target for formal verification. Rather than formalizing an entire chapter of Euclidean geometry, we may only need to formalize a small collection of elementary results.
